# A Corporate Data Scientist Runs for Office: Broad Listening on a Shoestring Budget

## Introduction: A Corporate Data Scientist Is Running for Office
My name is Kazuya Obanayama, and I ran in the 2025 Tokyo Metropolitan Assembly election for the Musashino City district with official endorsement from the political group Saisei no Michi.

In the spring of 2025, I entered the world of politics through an unexpected turn of events. I learned on YouTube that a political party was recruiting candidates, and I applied thinking it would at least be a good way to start learning about politics. To my surprise, I was actually endorsed as a candidate. While continuing to work my day job as a company employee, I suddenly found myself in a whirlwind of learning the basics of government and local assemblies and trying to put into my own words the issues I believed needed to be addressed.

Among those issues, one I knew I had to take up was healthcare. I work for a pharmaceutical company and have long been involved in healthcare and medicines, so the issue of medical costs has always been central in my field of vision—both in terms of the sustainability of the system and in terms of day-to-day realities on the ground. In an aging society with a declining birthrate, greater efficiency is unavoidable. But healthcare cannot be discussed only as a matter of cost optimization. Because it concerns how people live and their dignity, questions such as what to prioritize and how much society should support inevitably involve the will of the public. That is why I wanted to incorporate as wide a range of public views as possible into policy language.

What drew my attention at that point was the idea of “broad listening,” which was becoming a topic of discussion at the time. But for a first-time candidate challenging an election while holding down a corporate job, with limited time and money, spending heavily on research was simply not realistic. So I decided to draw on my professional background in data analysis and attempt a low-budget form of broad listening using free tools and generative AI. In this article, I will introduce that process of trial and error and how I put it into practice.


## 1. Why I Wanted to Listen to Public Views on Medical Costs
One of the triggers that led me to take up medical costs head-on as a policy issue was the suspension of medical services at Kichijoji Minami Hospital. Kichijoji, in Musashino City, is often named as one of the most desirable places to live. It is busy, vibrant, and relatively lively even by the standards of the Tokyo metropolitan area. Yet in Kichijoji, a hospital that had supported emergency care in the area fell into financial distress and suspended medical services starting in October 2024.

I took this development seriously because it was not simply a story about one hospital facing financial difficulties. For many years, people have talked about the harsh financial conditions facing medical institutions. But what shocked me was that this was not happening in a rural municipality struggling with weak finances. It was happening in an urban area with comparatively healthy public finances, and in a way visible to many people. It made me recognize that even in metropolitan areas, the foundations of community healthcare can be shaken—and that this is a question tied to the sustainability of Japan’s healthcare delivery system as a whole.

Rising medical costs, and the sustainability of the healthcare delivery system that depends on them, are not challenges unique to Japan. In many countries, efforts have advanced to reduce waste and duplication while preserving quality and access through measures such as data integration and telemedicine. Japan, too, has accumulated similar initiatives, but major barriers remain, including data standards, operational burdens, and coordination among stakeholders. The reality is that having the technology alone is not enough to move things forward.

One especially striking point for me was the example of places like Taiwan, which have succeeded in improving healthcare efficiency by building systems that allow information to be referenced across medical institutions under a single-insurer framework. Looking only at the component technologies, the gap with Japan is not all that large. And yet the difference in actual outcomes has been substantial. What this suggests is that the problem is not simply a lack of technology, but the design of the social systems and operational frameworks needed to make use of it. In recent years especially, advances in AI have made it increasingly common for technology to move ahead first and for institutions and operations to scramble after it. I believe this trend will only accelerate.

At the same time, there is no single correct answer to the question of how healthcare should be supported, what should be prioritized, and what should be changed in the name of efficiency. I can understand the argument that economic rationality should be strengthened. But healthcare is not just a cost; it is also a human endeavor bound up with life and dignity. That is why we cannot reach conclusions by looking only at the sustainability of the system, nor can we discuss the issue on ideals alone. What is needed, I felt, was to grasp as broadly as possible what people are anxious about, where they feel unfairness, and what they believe must be protected. So I decided to begin by listening to people’s views on the issue of medical costs.


## 2. What I Aimed For, and What I Gave Up
The first thing I decided for this broad listening effort was that I would **not aim for a representative quantitative survey**. Collecting data on a scale large enough to achieve representativeness would have required substantial expense. Since I was trying to find a way to run for office while continuing to work at a company, I concluded that activities requiring major spending were not the right choice. Moreover, even if I had spent money to use social media data, problems would still remain in terms of the representativeness of political opinion. There are biases inherent to digital spaces and platform algorithms, as well as issues such as duplicate posting.

So this time, rather than measuring the balance of support and opposition, I narrowed the goal to understanding the range of issues being raised and the language in which people were talking about them. Once I accepted bias and gave up on representativeness, it became possible to use free-response answers collected through Google Forms—a low-cost method that could be launched immediately.


## 3. How I Collected the Data and Prepared It
Here I explain how I collected free-response comments on medical costs and prepared them in a form suitable for analysis. For this project, I used Google Forms and collected responses anonymously around May 2025 through calls for participation on social media platforms such as X and YouTube. There was only one question, and it was open-ended: “Given that medical costs are expected to continue rising, what measures do you think are necessary?” I limited it to a single question primarily to keep the cost of participation as low as possible for respondents. I also wanted to avoid steering the discussion by adding more questions. With a free-response format, I believed I could avoid narrowing what voters chose to bring up on their own.

That said, I later learned that free-response formats also have their difficulties. In the course of later campaign activities, several people I spoke with face-to-face told me, “I meant to answer, but I gave up because I didn’t know what to write.” I found that reaction very interesting, because in conversation those same people were able to express their views quite clearly. While I wanted to avoid leading respondents, it also seemed true that people can find it easier to put their thoughts into words when they have other people’s opinions or some kind of prompt to work from. Opinions are not always sitting in people’s minds in fully formed shape from the start; sometimes they emerge through the setting or through supporting lines of thought. That was something I came to feel strongly.

In preprocessing, I first performed data cleaning. Responses that were clearly meaningless, such as “aaaaaa,” or extremely short responses that appeared to be input mistakes, were mechanically extracted based on character count and repetition, then checked visually and excluded. This part could certainly be automated, but since the number of responses was not enormous, I believed there was value in having a human look through the data once at the outset and develop a feel for it. In fact, even just reading some of the responses during preprocessing made it clear that some people had written with tremendous intensity. Coming into contact with that intensity was important, I think, in taking responsibility for the analysis that followed.

The next issue I tackled was how to handle responses containing multiple opinions. In free-response data, it is not unusual for one respondent to include several proposals or points at once. If such responses are treated as single blocks, later clustering and summarization can mix issues together and make the results harder to interpret. So, drawing on code from TTTC (Talk to the City, a public listening tool developed in Taiwan), I split responses during preprocessing into units of individual opinions. But splitting alone is not enough. If you look only at the segmented opinions afterward, you can lose the original context and misread them. For that reason, I made sure each opinion retained the ID of its original response so that I could always return to the full free-response text when needed.

I then conducted two kinds of analysis on the prepared free-response data. One focused on the spread of “opinions and proposals.” By grouping similar opinions and proposals together, I tried to get a bird’s-eye view of what kinds of issues were present. The other focused on the “target of emotion” and the “type of emotion” behind them. I chose to do this second analysis because, in my past experience in fields such as marketing, understanding not only the claims people make on the surface but also what they are feeling and toward what often greatly improves the resolution of one’s understanding. For example, even when two people both say “the burden should be reduced,” the explanation and dialogue required will differ depending on whether that view stems from distrust of the system, anxiety about the future, or a sense of unfairness.

For the analysis, I used the OpenAI API. There were 889 free-response submissions in total. After excluding 7 clearly noisy responses, I split the remainder into units of opinion and analyzed them. I treat the number of responses not as evidence of representativeness, but as supporting information for organizing the issues.

### 3-1. Did I Try Dimensionality Reduction, and Why Didn’t I Make It Central?
When people think of broad listening analysis, many probably imagine scatterplots that place large numbers of opinions in a two-dimensional space. In this project, however, I ultimately decided not to use two- or three-dimensional visualizations as the primary interpretive tool. That was because my goal was not to estimate the proportions of opinions, but to understand what kinds of issues were being discussed and in what language. Also, if I wanted to examine the distance relationships between clusters, I could do so either by calculating distances directly or by reading the cluster summaries themselves, so there was not much analytical advantage.

More than that, in discussions with voters and supporters, I found scatterplots costly to explain and difficult to connect to concrete discussion. By contrast, a simple table showing who was talking about what and with what kind of emotion made it easier to stay on track and move forward with both hypotheses and dialogue. For that reason, I prioritized reading summary texts and emotion categories, and did not use scatterplots visualizing the clusters.


## 4. How I Read the Spread of Opinions
To analyze the spread of opinions, I took the texts that had been broken down into units of opinion during preprocessing, converted them into vectors, then applied dimensionality reduction and clustering before summarizing each grouping. At the same time, I kept in mind a familiar limitation of this kind of analysis: the groupings produced can change easily with even small changes to algorithms or parameters. So I prioritized whether the resulting issue map of the full set of responses could be drawn in a way that felt natural and coherent.

I tried several algorithms and parameter settings, compared the summaries of each resulting cluster against the original responses, and returned to the source text whenever something felt off. I saved the trial conditions and looked for settings that converged while preserving the basic structure of the issues even when conditions changed. The summaries were treated only as signposts to help a human reader grasp the overall picture.


## 5. How I Read the Flow of Emotion
Next, in order to read the emotions behind the collected opinions, I used an LLM to extract candidate emotion targets and emotion types from the texts expressing each opinion. I then divided the data into chunks and carried out multi-stage categorization and integration. After that, I determined which categories each response belonged to, allowing multiple assignments where appropriate, and then used the LLM again to check whether those assignments were explicitly supported by the text and did not rely too heavily on unstated inference. Finally, I performed sampling checks on the original responses classified into each category, and when I found items that did not fit well, I redefined the categories and reran the process. I was not able to verify every single item perfectly, but I refined the definitions to the point where I could responsibly explain, “This category refers to this kind of text,” and then created an overview of the flow of emotions.


## 6. What Emerged
I collected 889 free-response submissions in this project. However, what I present here is not a quantitative conclusion about the distribution of opinion among voters as a whole. Rather, it is an organized account of what kinds of issues emerged from the collected free responses and what kinds of emotional currents could be seen behind them. In this section, I first present the results themselves; in the next chapter, I will consider their meaning and limitations in more depth. The responses were collected anonymously, and because of the way the survey was introduced and the limits of space, I do not include the original texts or the full analytical details here.

### 6-1. What the Spread of Opinions Revealed
First, I organized what kinds of “opinions and proposals” appeared in the free responses. What matters here is not which opinions or proposals were most common, but into what kinds of domains the issue of medical costs was being divided in people’s minds. Looking across the whole set, I found opinions in areas such as upstream measures like prevention and screening, efficiency measures such as healthcare DX and data integration, redesign of burdens and benefits, reform of the healthcare delivery system, and end-of-life care and ACP (advance care planning).

| A   | Prevention and Health Promotion | Reduce disease onset through health education, exercise, nutrition, smoking cessation, etc. |
| B   | Checkups and Screening        | Reconsider target ages and frequency to balance early detection and cost-effectiveness |
| C   | Healthcare DX and Data Integration | Reduce duplicate tests and duplicate prescriptions while improving quality and efficiency |

When organized this way, it becomes clear that people were trying to answer the single question of rising medical costs from quite different starting points. Some emphasized prevention before illness occurs, while others argued that the rules of the system itself should be reconsidered. Still others raised issues such as the structure of healthcare supply or the nature of end-of-life care—topics that clearly involve value judgments beyond simple cost cutting. At the very least, these free responses suggest that the issue of medical costs is being understood not as a single policy question, but as a bundle of multiple issue clusters.

### 6-2. What the Flow of Emotion Revealed
Next, I organized where the emotions behind these opinions seemed to be directed and what kinds of emotions they were. What I am dealing with here is not a definitive judgment about the inner feelings of individual respondents. Rather, I have grouped into categories the recurring emotional directions and emotional types that appeared in the free responses. Overall, what emerged were currents such as anger over perceived unfairness and overuse, distrust and wariness, concern for dignity at the end of life, hope for prevention and healthcare DX, and tension surrounding the social safety net.

These results suggest that even within the single theme of medical costs, a great variety of emotions and emotional targets are involved. By separating out what people are feeling and toward what, I think it became easier to see the direction of public sentiment in ways that are hard to capture through surface-level support or opposition alone.

That is the outline of the results from this analysis. In the next chapter, I would like to examine more deeply how these results should be interpreted, and also the differences between voices gathered digitally and those I actually heard in person on the street.


## 7. What I Learned by Doing It
Here I would like to reflect on what this experiment taught me. The free responses did not uncover a huge number of radically new policy ideas. Rather, their value lay in allowing me to survey what kinds of issues people associated with the single theme of medical costs, and in what combinations they talked about them. Prevention, screening, healthcare DX, institutional redesign, healthcare delivery systems, and end-of-life care are, in one sense, issues that have already been articulated in society as part of debates over the healthcare system. Even so, there was real value in seeing what kinds of emotions accompanied which issues.

In particular, reading the data by separating “the target of emotion” from “the type of emotion” made it easier to see the values and tensions behind people’s claims. Being able to distinguish currents such as intergenerational unfairness, distrust of the system, ambivalence toward welfare, and hope for new technologies such as prevention and healthcare DX was one of the major gains of this analysis.

Another especially important lesson from this effort was the strong impression I received that the distribution of opinions gathered digitally differed quite clearly from the distribution of opinions I heard directly from voters while standing on the street. On the digital side, voices about the merits of institutional design, fairness, anger over overuse, and hope for new technologies stood out. On the street, by contrast, what left the strongest impression were more immediate, life-centered concerns: fears that welfare support might be weakened, or comments such as, “I understand the need for efficiency, but I’m worried whether I can keep up with a system built on digital assumptions.” At least from my own experience on the street this time, voices actively calling for bold, painful institutional reform were in the minority.

I do not think this gap can be explained simply by differences in digital literacy. **Opinions are not fixed things that exist in fully formed shape from the start; how they emerge changes depending on the setting in which people are placed.** In face-to-face settings, people may find it harder to voice opinions that could hurt someone, while at the same time some only manage to sort out their thoughts and put them into words through conversation itself. In digital settings, by contrast, it may be easier to articulate criticism of institutions or bold proposals, but people who stumble at the very stage of putting thoughts into words may be more likely to be left out. Through this experience, I came to feel that broad listening is better understood not as a method completed within a single channel, but as a practice of layering together information obtained from multiple channels and multiple segments, and reading even the gaps between them. Digital methods are strong at broadening the range of issues; street-level listening is strong at capturing urgency, hesitation, and the process by which thoughts become language. What matters is not choosing one or the other, but treating the difference itself as an object of interpretation.

The technologies related to broad listening are also continuing to change along with the evolution of LLMs. The approach I used this time may remain useful in broad terms, but its concrete implementation will surely be replaced by better methods over time. In fact, the barriers to data collection are gradually falling, and the amount of related open-source software is increasing. Even so, I still feel it will take a little more time before broad listening can be implemented in a way that both preserves some degree of representativeness and is easy for anyone to use casually. In particular, having seen in street-level conversations how the very act of putting opinions into words can be supported, I believe that even in digital settings, it will be important not merely to collect opinions but to support the process by which opinions become language.

In that sense, I see two major directions for the future of broad listening. One is to further formalize the kind of “bias-aware use” I attempted here. We need frameworks that layer together multiple channels and multiple results, and that also read the reasons for the gaps between them. The other is to create mechanisms that support voters in putting their opinions into words even in digital settings. What is needed is not technology that has AI speak for people or make decisions for them, but technology that reduces the friction in the process by which opinions arise, become language, reach others, and connect to public discussion.

## 8. After the Election, and What Comes Next
The 2025 Tokyo Metropolitan Assembly election that I challenged ended in defeat. Even so, this campaign—my first direct encounter with the world of politics, undertaken as someone who had worked in data science in the private sector and who also made use of LLMs—was an enormously valuable learning opportunity for me. It allowed me to hear voters’ voices directly, learn from the ingenuity of those who came before me, and revisit in the setting of real politics a question that had long concerned me in my work: the point of contact between technology and society.

What I felt after the election was that becoming a politician is not the only route to changing society. Of course, there is great significance in running for office and seeking to realize policy. At the same time, I came to believe that directly working on social problems, or building systems that gather and structure people’s opinions, is also an important form of political participation. In order not to let the concerns and lessons I gained through the election end as a one-time experience, and to continue returning them to society, I founded the general incorporated association Local Plus with colleagues. Going forward, I hope to continue connecting the experience I developed in the private sector with the worlds of government and civic activity.

Through this experience, I came to feel that broad listening is not simply a “technology for collecting large volumes of opinion,” but a technology for helping people put their thoughts into words and connect them to discussion. Precisely because AI technology is advancing and machines are able to do more and more, the human work of putting into words what we value and what we choose will become even more important. I believe there will be growing room for people in data science and engineering to engage with politics and government. A way of building a career that circulates among the private sector, politics, and government can bring innovation both to individuals and to society.

Finally, I would like once again to express my gratitude to everyone who shared their views through Google Forms, to everyone who spoke to me directly on the street or at campaign speeches, and to the 4,727 people who entrusted me with their vote. The opinions and feelings I received did not end with this campaign; they continue to live on as the starting point for what I do next. Going forward, I hope to keep building on both learning and practice while standing at the intersection of technology and society.
