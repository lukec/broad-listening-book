## Cybozu’s Approach — Connecting Hard-to-See Voices to the Next Discussion

When people hold a meeting, it can feel as though everyone has talked things through together. In reality, however, the opinions that stand out tend to be those of people who are good at speaking, while unspoken anxieties and half-formed uncertainties are less likely to be shared. And yet it is precisely in those voices that we often find the clues to blind spots that are currently being overlooked.

Broad listening makes it possible to gather those hard-to-see voices as widely as possible and reduce what gets missed. The important thing is not to force a black-and-white conclusion right away. It is to make visible where there is common ground, where there are differences, and what issues still remain unresolved.

Cybozu’s example shows clearly how this way of thinking can be used inside a company. It suggests that broad listening is not only for politics, but can also be useful in familiar settings such as organizations and media.

## A New Form of Workshop Using AI

In August 2025, Cybozu held an internal workshop in which AI supported participants’ discussions.
This initiative was later published as a web article and a YouTube video.[^idobata]

The tool used here was the “Idobata System,” which was also explained in the TODO chapter.
This is a deliberation support tool developed within the Digital Democracy 2030 community. At the time, Team Mirai (meaning “Team Future”) had set up the online environment and was using it for things like online talks with supporters. For this workshop, a dedicated environment was built for Cybozu. This entire effort was carried out by commissioning Shutaro Aoyama, who had been involved in developing the Idobata System, to organize the workshop, working in coordination with Cybozu’s engineers.

The theme of the workshop was “Promoting AI Use Within the Company.” About 50 employees gathered in a hybrid format, both in person and online, and discussed how to think about this topic across differences in department, job type, and position. Participants came from a wide range of roles, including information systems, customer-facing teams, sales, development, and HR.

### Starting with the Sticky-Note Method

![A conventional sticky-note exercise conducted in a Cybozu workshop. Yellow, pink, and blue sticky notes are arranged on large sheets of paper on the table, and participants have handwritten concerns such as “strengthen internal training” and “share success stories.” The five-person groups were given 15 minutes to organize their ideas, and the exercise was designed to let participants experience the limits of trying to structure diverse opinions in a short time.](images/10_03_cybozu_fusennwork.jpg)

This workshop did not begin with AI. First, participants were divided into groups of five. In a familiar workshop format, each person wrote down their thoughts on sticky notes within a set time, and then shared them orally within the group. Online participants did the same using sticky notes on the online whiteboard system Miro, and then talked in groups using breakout rooms in Zoom.

The purpose was to lower the psychological barrier to writing things down by using a familiar method rather than asking people to enter their thoughts directly into an unfamiliar system. Of course, what counts as a “familiar method” will differ from one workshop participant to another. Miro and Zoom could be treated as familiar methods here because this was a corporate workshop, where participants’ IT literacy could be assumed. That would not necessarily be the case in a workshop run by a local government and open freely to the general public.

In this session, participants spent five minutes writing and ten minutes sharing. Comments included things like, “Shouldn’t we put more effort into internal training?” and “Shouldn’t we share more success stories and practical tips?” Even with this method, being required to spend five minutes focused on thinking does create an opportunity to put thoughts into words. At the same time, the pressure of having to explain those thoughts to the rest of the team after five minutes can be difficult, especially for people who are not comfortable verbalizing their ideas.

Experiencing these “limits firsthand” helped participants understand the advantages of AI support.

### AI Digs Deeper with the “Idobata System”

![A Cybozu participant using Idobata on a laptop. On the left side of the screen is the “Idobata Meeting” interface, and on the right is a chat window with the AI. A summary of discussion points has been generated, including “I want to understand the current challenges in AI use and concrete solutions,” showing how the AI structures issues in real time based on participants’ responses. Traces of the earlier sticky-note exercise remain on the table.](images/10_03_cybozu_idobata_pc.jpg)

The next tool used was the “Idobata System.” In this system, the AI responds to what participants write with follow-up questions. By answering those questions, participants can keep digging deeper through conversation, which encourages them to put their thoughts into words. Using this system shifts the mental model from “sitting alone at a desk and writing” to “having a conversation.”

There is real value in the fact that the conversation partner is not another human being, but AI. Some people are more likely than others, when speaking to another person, to think things like, “I shouldn’t dominate the conversation,” or “What will this person think if I say this?” People with a stronger tendency in that direction are more inhibited about expressing their thoughts than those with a weaker tendency. When the other party is AI, they can speak one-sidedly and freely about what they think without worrying so much.

The fact that this tendency differs from person to person may be easier to understand if we look at the harm caused by the opposite pattern. In meetings where several people are listening, if one person talks endlessly without caring what the audience thinks, the setting stops being a place for deliberation and turns into that person’s solo performance. Many readers have probably had the experience of being frustrated by someone like that.

Speech is a weak communication channel because only one person can output it at a time. In settings where speech is the only communication channel, if one person launches into a spoken monologue, the other participants lose their opportunity to communicate. One reason workshops so often use writing exercises is that communication channels that allow information to be output in parallel are valuable.

### Bringing Everyone’s Views Together to Identify Agreement and Difference

Dialogue with AI encourages people to put their thoughts into words more effectively than struggling alone. In the Idobata System, the sharing of those results is also supported. In a meeting with 50 participants, even if each person could share their thoughts in just one minute, that would still take 50 minutes in total. In reality, listening to and understanding the thoughts of the other 49 people and then expressing your own in one minute when your turn comes is almost impossibly difficult for most people. As a result, under previous methods, most participants’ views would go unshared.

In the Idobata System, AI reads the chat logs from all 50 participants, analyzes them in a matter of minutes, and generates a report on what kinds of views people hold. It can then share the most frequently mentioned themes, along with points of agreement and disagreement on those themes, so that participants can continue the discussion.

When points of agreement are visible, it becomes possible to allocate time more effectively: “We already share this point, so let’s spend our time on the issues where views diverge.” The value of broad listening lies not only in gathering many voices, but also in making it possible to design where to spend the cost of discussion afterward.

What the Idobata System does is collect raw material through one-on-one dialogue between AI and each participant, and then convert that material into a form that can be shared. It does not share each individual’s chat content as-is. Instead, the AI summarizes it and organizes it into commonalities and differences. Put another way, it is a mechanism for building a picture of the whole while still protecting each person’s honest feelings.

### Surfacing Issues, Not Conclusions

What matters here is that issues come first, not conclusions. When people rush to conclusions, moving forward often means sacrificing someone’s sense of understanding or acceptance. But if the issues are visible, the group can first share a picture of what exactly people disagree about.

For example, on the workshop theme of “promoting AI use within the company,” an internal Cybozu survey conducted in June 2025 found that 80% of the 1,118 respondents said they “use AI in daily life.” At the same time, many individuals still felt vague anxiety, such as “I’m not using it effectively at all” or “It feels like other companies are making much better use of it.”

As long as that anxiety remains vague, it is difficult to take action to improve things. But through the use of the Idobata System in this workshop, one source of that anxiety became clear: “It’s hard to tell what information can and cannot be entered into AI systems.”

For individuals, it is often difficult to put anxiety into words and communicate it to others. But once people first unload what they are feeling to AI, and the AI then organizes it, the result becomes easier to handle not as “one person’s feelings” but as a structure in which “many people share the same anxiety.” Participants themselves commented that they realized, “More people than I expected are thinking the same thing.”

### Vertical Facilitation and Horizontal Facilitation

Another point of disagreement that emerged was whether AI adoption should be driven top-down or through autonomous bottom-up action. This was especially interesting to the author.

In *Facilitating Breakthrough*, Adam Kahane explains that there are vertical and horizontal forms of facilitation.
The vertical form is top-down, with the boss making decisions. Things move quickly, but it can easily produce resistance and silence.
The horizontal form is flat and inclusive, but it can also lead to stagnation, with nothing being decided.
Cybozu has regarded vertical decision-making as undesirable and has worked to shift toward a horizontal model. Yet in the face of the external shock of the AI revolution, voices have emerged calling for strong vertical leadership.

The Idobata System is well suited to making overly vertical situations more horizontal, and it is likely to be useful for including people who feel alienated. At the same time, the system itself does not make decisions. Kahane argues that it is useful to move back and forth between vertical and horizontal modes. After listening broadly to many people’s views, some form of “leadership” is still needed to make decisions and move things forward. From this perspective, broad listening can be understood as a technology that enhances a leader’s capacity to understand others.

### Summarizing Actually Reduces the Risk of Missing Minority Views

During the Q&A for this event, someone asked, “Doesn’t AI summarization risk overlooking minority opinions?” This is a good opportunity to explain. Summarization compresses most strongly those opinion groups in which many people hold similar views. That means that if you are willing to spend the same amount of effort understanding opinions, AI summarization actually reduces the risk of missing minority views compared with reading comments at random.

Imagine a situation in which 10,000 people have submitted opinions, and 100 of them—1%—hold a minority view. If you randomly select and read 100 submissions, there is about a 37% chance that you will miss that minority view entirely. By contrast, if similar opinions are grouped together and ranked, and you read from the top, a 1% opinion will certainly appear within the top 100 and will not be missed. If we assume the distribution of opinions follows Zipf’s law, then a 1% opinion would likely rank within roughly the top 15. But if you randomly selected just 15 submissions, there would be about an 86% chance of missing it.

In reality, things are not even random—often only the views of the loudest people get through. That is precisely why it matters to give everyone an opportunity to speak through a parallel method and then summarize the results with AI rather than relying on random sampling. Doing so makes it easier to see opinions from more minority perspectives and increases the chance of noticing blind spots.

And this is not only a matter of probability. If AI can speed up the work of organizing the issues that concern large numbers of people, then meeting time that would previously have been consumed by that task can instead be directed toward minority issues that were often postponed with the excuse that “we’re too busy.”

In that sense, the Idobata System is less a tool for replacing meetings themselves than a tool for preparing the ground before decision-making. Once that groundwork has been done, the meetings and decisions that follow can become far more concrete and constructive.

## The Plurality Idea Behind It

Behind these efforts is the idea of *Plurality*. In May 2024, Audrey Tang and Glen Weyl published a book by that title, and in May 2025 the Japanese edition, *PLURALITY: Collaborative Technology and the Future of Democracy, Turning Conflict into Creativity*, was published by Cybozu Shiki Books.

Cybozu had long embraced the corporate philosophy of “building a society overflowing with teamwork.” This book aims to help people collaborate better through technology, making it highly compatible with Cybozu’s own philosophy.

In a promotional video for the book released in August 2025 on the YouTube channel *Yuru Computer Science Radio*, which has 280,000 subscribers,[^youtube] Polis was introduced as “the social network that could save the world,” drawing a major response with 280,000 views and more than 1,000 comments. In that video, one especially striking formulation was: “Existing social media makes you focus on individuals, but Polis makes you focus not on individuals, but on groups.” The author considers this an excellent insight.

When people perceive others as individuals, they can understand only a few dozen at most. But by perceiving them instead as a distribution of group opinions, attention can shift away from person-to-person disputes and toward caring for the landscape of collective views. The author sees broad listening as a technology for strengthening humanity’s overall capacity to understand groups, and believes that a humanity strengthened by this technology will go on to build organizations and societies unlike those we have known before.

## “Meetings That Don’t Force Black-and-White Answers” Expanded Beyond the Company

Since the Idobata workshop in August 2025, voices have emerged across many departments within Cybozu asking to use the Idobata System. Later tools such as Baisoku Kaigi, an AI-supported “fast-track meeting” tool, have also been put to use internally. A number of interesting developments have taken place that have not yet been made public outside the company.

At the same time, efforts also began to expand these initiatives beyond internal use.

On February 11, 2026, Cybozu launched a new YouTube channel, *Meetings That Don’t Force Black-and-White Answers*, in collaboration with pedantic, the company behind *Yuru Computer Science Radio*. The aim was to let more people experience the culture of “dialogue and discussion” that Cybozu values.

We believe that when people share the information that forms the basis of discussion—data, history, values, and positions—dialogue between people with different ways of thinking can move forward, and the process by which their views change becomes important. For that reason, this YouTube channel deliberately discusses topics with diverse opinions without forcing them into black-and-white conclusions, so that viewers can experience the interest and value of “taking in diverse opinions and thinking for yourself.”

The theme of the first video was: “Does remote work increase productivity, or decrease it?” Three speakers discussed the topic while indicating their degree of agreement on a lever ranging from 0% to 100%.

![alt text](images/09_03_shirokuro_kaigi_remote_work_debate.png)

What is distinctive about this format is that it does not reduce the discussion to a binary choice of “for” or “against.” If someone’s view changes during the discussion, the position of the lever changes as well, allowing viewers to watch in real time how opinions shift and are updated.

As of March 13, 2026, this first video had been viewed more than 180,000 times and had received over 950 comments. Those comments are collected through an API and then analyzed and visualized using “Public Listening AI,” which is being developed by the Digital Democracy 2030 community. As in the Bowling Green example introduced in Chapter 10, Section 3, the visualization uses JSON analysis data to represent the volume of opinions by the size of circles.

![alt text](images/09_03_shirokuro_kaigi_youtube_comments.png)

On the large-scale public infrastructure of YouTube, the speakers first share the issues and background knowledge, and viewers then write comments.
This is different from the kind of discussion on social media where people simply throw out whatever comes to mind. In the sense that participants form opinions after receiving a certain amount of information, it can be seen as one transformed version of deliberation moved onto video media.

## From Hard-to-See Voices to an Organization’s Next Step

What Cybozu’s efforts show is not that AI replaces human beings. Rather, it can take voices that have often been overlooked and shape them into something that can feed into the next discussion.

People often treat “a place to express opinions” and “a place to make decisions” as if they were the same thing. In reality, there is a great distance between the two. Unspoken anxieties, poorly organized discomfort, and minority concerns are all likely to fall away somewhere along that distance. What broad listening and systems like Idobata do is precisely to fill that gap.

First, they make it possible for each person to put what they are feeling into words in a safe way. Then they gather many people’s views and make commonalities and differences visible. In doing so, organizations become able to build the next discussion not around “the opinions of the people who argue most forcefully,” but around “how members are actually feeling.” This is also a way for members of an organization to understand themselves more accurately.

And this way of thinking can be extended to internal workshops, video media, and dialogue with local governments as well. Do not rush to black-and-white answers; first look at the distribution. Before rushing to conclusions, share a map of the issues. This attitude is needed not to avoid conflict, but to create better conflict and spark better discussion.

Broad listening is a technology for leveling the ground before deliberation begins. Turning hard-to-see voices into visible issues: Cybozu’s practice shows that this is no longer just an ideal, but is already becoming a practical method that can be used on the ground.
