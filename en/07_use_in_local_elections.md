# Chapter 7: Using Broad Listening in Local Elections

Local elections come with challenges that differ from those in national elections. In national politics, there are clear issues such as foreign policy, national security, and economic policy, and each party’s position is relatively easy to understand. In local elections, by contrast, the key issues are often harder to see, and it is not easy for candidates to grasp what residents are truly saying. The main clues usually come from supporters’ associations and the voices of backers and supporters, but those voices do not necessarily reflect the will of the entire community accurately. There is often a gap between the voices a candidate hears directly and what large numbers of residents actually want. What value, then, can broad listening offer in addressing these distinctive challenges of local elections?

This chapter introduces the experiences of three candidates who used broad listening.

Taisuke Ono, who won the Yatsushiro mayoral election in Kumamoto Prefecture, used Public Listening AI to ask broad questions about city government as a whole. In doing so, he overturned the assumption behind his campaign strategy that distrust of government was the only issue that mattered, and discovered strong public interest in the economy and culture as well.

Ryousuke Ide, who ran in the Tokyo Metropolitan Assembly election, also used Public Listening AI. By focusing his questions on a single issue—the Nakano Sunplaza—he drew out residents’ deeply held views and made a major shift in the redevelopment policy he had supported for many years.

Kazuya Obanayama, who ran in the same Tokyo Metropolitan Assembly election from the Musashino constituency, took a different approach. Instead of using Public Listening AI, he combined Google Forms with an LLM in a method of his own, putting broad listening into practice on a low budget while working as a corporate data scientist. He focused specifically on the policy issue of medical expenses, seeking to understand not only the range of opinions but also the emotional currents behind them.

These three contrasting approaches show that the effectiveness of broad listening depends on what is asked, how it is asked, and what tools are used. At the same time, they make clear that broad listening has the power to break through politicians’ assumptions.
