# Boots Inc.

By Keisuke Ariga (Representative Director, Boots Inc.)

English translation by Luke Closs

## Introduction

This article introduces the current state and future outlook of broad listening from a practical, real-world perspective, drawing on support cases handled by Boots Inc., which helps local governments, companies, and organizations implement broad listening, as well as the insights gained from those efforts.

## About Boots Inc.

Boots Inc. (Representative: Keisuke Ariga) is a management consulting firm that supports private companies, local governments, and other organizations in transforming their businesses and updating operational processes through DX promotion support, including the use of AI.

In recent years, digital technologies such as AI have attracted significant attention. At the same time, not every company or organization is fully benefiting from them in practice. One reason, in our view, is that many organizations remain focused only on how to use individual tools, without establishing a full problem-solving cycle that begins with identifying issues in frontline operations and continues through solution design, implementation, verification, and improvement. Grounded in a practical understanding of technology, our company provides services with a firm commitment to driving change in organizational workflows, always starting from business and operational challenges themselves.

Our main support offerings can be broadly divided into three areas.  
First, consulting services centered on strategy consulting and operations consulting.  
Second, digital talent development services through training programs and workshops.  
Third, support services for the development and implementation of AI applications, data analysis, and related work.

Broad listening support falls under the third category, data analysis support. In practice, however, broad listening is not something that produces fixed results in every situation simply by following a predetermined analytical procedure. Rather, it provides useful insights for operations by consistently working backward from the user’s operational challenges and the desired form of dialogue with residents, and then designing and carrying out data preparation, collection, and analytical methods accordingly. For that reason, while there are some cases in which we are commissioned only to analyze data prepared by the client, in many cases we provide end-to-end consulting support, from issue clarification and requirements definition to discussions on data collection methods and the execution of analysis itself.

In addition, we also support the development of internal capabilities for analysis using various tools, including *Kocho AI*, developed under *Digital Democracy 2030* and released as open-source software (OSS), by helping clients build the necessary environment and develop personnel.

## Broad Listening Support Cases (Publicly Available Examples)

We have supported many organizations, especially local governments, that have consulted us about introducing broad listening. Publicly available examples can be found on the websites of the respective local governments listed below.

All of the following cases were carried out in 2025, and all used *Kocho AI*, developed under *Digital Democracy 2030*, as the broad listening tool. These were pilot initiatives intended to efficiently analyze free-response comments collected by local governments from residents and put them to meaningful use.

### Shibuya City (Tokyo)

Broad listening was conducted using response data (6,037 responses) from the free-response section of the resident awareness survey conducted in fiscal 2024 (question: “Please tell us anything you feel about Shibuya City as it is today, including things you are satisfied or dissatisfied with.”).

According to materials published on the Shibuya City website, the analysis went beyond presenting “the overall picture of residents’ opinions” and also included analysis under specific conditions such as “analysis by age group” and “analysis by geographic area,” allowing the city to examine the views of particular resident groups in greater depth.

### Iwate Prefecture

Broad listening was conducted using the following data:
- Comments received from prefectural residents during the “Workshop on Thinking About Happiness” held in fiscal 2024
- Comments collected in fiscal 2023 through opinion-gathering from children (elementary through high school students)

### Utazu Town (Kagawa Prefecture)

Broad listening was conducted using 396 responses to the free-response question in the resident survey carried out during the formulation of the Second Utazu Town Comprehensive Plan in March 2024: “Please freely write any opinions or suggestions you may have regarding what you would like the Utazu Town administration to do in the future or about community development.”

## Insights from Support Cases in Local Governments

### Challenges Faced by Local Governments

In general, understanding residents’ voices is a fundamental prerequisite for virtually all public administration activities. For that reason, local governments collect opinions through a wide range of opportunities—public comments, resident surveys, questionnaires, dialogue meetings, workshops, and more—and then organize and analyze the information gathered.

However, when it comes to residents’ free-response “voices” collected at scales ranging from hundreds to tens of thousands of entries (and in some municipalities, even larger), the full process of reading every response, organizing and analyzing them, compiling a report, and then reflecting the findings in policy measures and administrative planning places a very heavy burden on local government staff and creates challenges from multiple perspectives.

#### From the perspective of how public employees work

The time required for routine analytical work increases, adding to the burden on public employees. At the same time, it becomes harder for them to devote sufficient time to higher-value work that is more essential to their role, such as considering and improving public measures based on residents’ voices and engaging in dialogue with residents.

#### From the perspective of agility in public administration

The lead time from collecting residents’ voices to reflecting them in policy becomes longer, making it harder for residents to feel that their voices are actually reaching the government. In addition, it becomes difficult to connect residents’ voices in real time to policy discussions on current administrative issues, making it harder to design a dialogue process that feels genuinely grounded in people’s lived realities.

#### From the perspective of fewer opportunities to collect residents’ voices

Because analysis and consolidation require so much effort, local governments may be unable to create many opportunities for collecting opinions in the first place, and resident surveys and questionnaires may tend to rely mainly on multiple-choice questions that are easier to analyze.

In addition, rather than conducting large-scale resident surveys, governments may more often rely on limited events such as dialogue meetings and workshops to hear residents’ voices. But because the number of speakers in such settings is constrained by the design of the event, it is difficult to capture a broad overall picture of residents’ views. This creates a barrier to understanding the so-called “silent majority” in a nuanced way and appropriately reflecting those views in public policy.

### Insights from Broad Listening Cases

Through the cases to date, a variety of insights have emerged regarding the usefulness of broad listening and expectations for its future development. It is also expected to offer at least a partial solution to the kinds of operational challenges faced by local governments described above.

First, in terms of analytical efficiency, broad listening has been highly valued for its ability to quickly classify (label) large numbers of opinions, organize relationships among them, and summarize them, making it possible to immediately grasp the overall picture from a bird’s-eye view. Analytical work that previously took several weeks to several months can now be completed in a matter of minutes to tens of minutes with comparable output. If analysis can be performed this quickly, it opens the door to designing richer dialogue processes, such as follow-up surveys that dig deeper based on the results or dialogue meetings with residents conducted while presenting the analysis findings.

The accuracy and quality of the analytical results have also been evaluated as being at a level suitable for practical use, which can be seen as evidence of the maturity of broad listening technology. Broad listening has also been recognized as making it easier to identify latent issues and needs that might previously have remained buried, while helping to organize key points for policy consideration. As a result, it is expected to create value in a wide range of administrative settings, including survey analysis, aggregation of public comments, and organization of residents’ opinions.

Furthermore, visualizing analytical results in charts and plots is expected to make it easier to share findings within local governments and explain them externally, thereby contributing to higher-quality discussion.

In response to these positive evaluations, some local governments have also begun considering improvements to how surveys are conducted and how data is collected, redesigning these processes on the assumption that broad listening will be used. Some have already started reviewing workflow design more broadly, including surrounding processes such as online surveys and digitization using OCR.

At the same time, several challenges have also become clear with respect to implementation.

One is the need to develop internal human resources in order to internalize broad listening operations.  
A certain level of digital literacy is required on the user side among staff, including the ability to set analysis conditions and configure prompts for AI processing. That said, some local governments have already reached the point where staff themselves have become proficient and are using these tools effectively. We believe that securing personnel with basic IT engineering skills within local governments—even if they are not specialized AI engineers—is important not only for broad listening but also for tackling the many kinds of workflow updates required in the AI era.

It has also been pointed out that, depending on the content of the analysis and the characteristics of the target data, the current analytical process used by *Kocho AI* may not always be the optimal one. This has led to expectations for the expansion of technical methods related to broad listening (see below).

In this way, while some challenges remain, practical pilots and implementations have given frontline users a tangible sense of what broad listening can do, and many ideas have emerged regarding the expansion of use cases (for example, various planning processes, large-scale surveys, routine inquiry analysis, and more). Further expansion of broad listening use cases and deeper adoption in public administration are therefore widely anticipated.

## Future Prospects for Broad Listening

Based on broad listening use to date in local governments, companies, and organizations, this section discusses future prospects from two perspectives: technology and application.

### [Technology] Expansion of Technical Methods

One important perspective in considering the future development of broad listening is the enhancement of technical methods. Broadly speaking, this has two dimensions. One is “how to collect voices,” and the other is “how to analyze the voices that have been collected.”

#### Methods for collecting voices

A key point in conducting broad listening is to gather a wide range of voices without depending on any single collection method. By handling diverse channels in an integrated way—such as online surveys, social media, paper questionnaires, and street interviews—it becomes possible to collect broader, more balanced voices that are closer to the overall population. Recently, some local governments have also begun collecting input through official apps and LINE channels, reflecting ongoing efforts to lower the barriers to making one’s voice heard.

There is also room for progress in methods for collecting higher-quality individual opinions. One example is the so-called depth interview approach, in which opinions are explored more deeply through dialogue with AI. If information such as background, reasons, and specific circumstances—things that cannot be fully captured through simple free-response comments—can be elicited in a conversational format, it becomes possible to obtain higher-quality input for policy consideration. High-quality input from residents, stakeholders, and experts who are particularly affected by a given policy issue may contain important insights even if the number of responses is small. Drawing useful lessons from such information is expected to help refine policy.

#### Methods for analyzing collected voices

At the same time, analytical methods also need to be strengthened.

In *Kocho AI*, the approach used is to convert input text into numerical data through vectorization and then analyze it quantitatively in order to group opinions through clustering. This is highly effective for efficiently identifying broad trends from large volumes of opinions. On the other hand, it is not necessarily a universal solution when it comes to issues such as whether the grouping criteria feel convincing (for example, whether they align with the local government’s operational context), how to interpret relationships among opinion groups, or how to extract minority opinions that nonetheless contain important insights. For that reason, we believe that analytical methods need to be expanded in ways that reflect the purpose of the analysis and the characteristics of the target data.

For example, analysis design tailored to the objective is essential for understanding the structure of policy issues, organizing opinions by stakeholder group, or extracting expert views. Rather than relying on a single tool, what is needed is an environment in which multiple analytical methods can be combined and selected according to the need.

What matters in broad listening is not a simple “greater or smaller number,” as in majority rule, but rather extracting voices that offer insight and learning, and organizing the structure among opinions in order to generate useful findings. To make that possible, further expansion of the technical methods surrounding broad listening is highly desirable.

### [Application] Broader Adoption, Use, and Value Creation

From the application perspective, the key issue for broad listening is how to embed it into a broader workflow so that it produces concrete results.

The first point to keep in mind here is that broad listening should not be treated in isolation as merely an “AI tool” or an “analytical task.” The process of collecting, analyzing, and visualizing the voices of residents or users is only one part of the overall decision-making process. What matters is that it functions as a genuine, human-centered dialogue process with citizens.

More specifically, what is needed is a loop in which: (1) voices are collected through diverse means; (2) broad listening is used to organize and extract opinion trends and key issues; (3) the extracted issues are examined in forums of assemblies, government, and management, and reflected in policies and improvements; and (4) the results are fed back through public communication and tangible service improvements. As this cycle becomes recognized, residents and users can come to feel that “speaking up actually leads to change,” which in turn encourages further participation and expression. Broad listening should therefore be positioned as a foundational technology for accelerating this cycle and enabling it to function consistently.

Some local governments have already begun discussing the process as a whole in this way, and going forward, it is expected that more refined implementation and operating models will emerge through the accumulation of such cases.

Broad listening is also attracting attention not only in politics and public administration but also among private companies and various organizations as a useful approach.

For example, in private companies, relevant themes include marketing (customer understanding and product improvement), customer success (identifying issues, improving services, and increasing satisfaction), and internal communication (identifying workplace issues, improving organizational design, and raising employee satisfaction). In industry associations and similar bodies, examples are also emerging in which broad listening is used to consolidate stakeholder opinions and reflect them in policy proposals and related efforts.

The importance of carefully understanding the needs of customers and stakeholders, extracting useful insights, and rapidly reflecting them in operational processes is common across many companies and organizations. But as the number of people involved grows and needs become more complex, conventional methods alone become insufficient. Expectations are therefore rising for broad listening as a technology that can bridge that gap.
