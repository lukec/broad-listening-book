# Chapter 4 The Spread of Broad Listening Within Japan

## The Democratic Party for the People Uses an "Issue Map" in Questioning the Government in the Japanese Parliament

### Making the Voices of the "Employment Ice Age" Generation Visible and Pointing Out Policy Bias

On March 7, 2025, a groundbreaking event took place in the House of Councillors Budget Committee. Takae Ito, a lawmaker from the Democratic Party for the People, presented an "issue map" created with TTTC as supporting material and used it to question Prime Minister Ishiba. This marked the first time in Japan's Parliament that the results of broad listening were used as the basis for questioning the government.

The issue Ito focused on was the everyday challenges faced by people who came of age during Japan's long hiring slump of the late 1990s and early 2000s, now mostly in their 40s. Opinions were gathered through multiple channels, including the telephone hotline "Kokumin Denwa" (050-1722-5930), Google Forms, social media, and comments posted during YouTube livestreams, and then analyzed using TTTC. The results were visualized in a scatterplot, which was then used as material in the parliamentary questioning.

![TTTC scatterplot used by the Democratic Party for the People in parliamentary questioning: visualizing opinions on the life challenges faced by people in their 40s. Diverse issues such as employment, retirement benefits, housing, caregiving, and loneliness/social isolation are clustered.](images/04_02_kokumin_tttc_sanpuzu.png)
*Figure: An issue map generated by TTTC and presented by Takae Ito in the House of Councillors Budget Committee (Source: Democratic Party for the People, March 7, 2025)*

In this scatterplot, each dot represents one person's opinion. While showing the material, Ito asked the following:

> "Prime Minister, please look at the material. Each of these dots represents one person's opinion. Employment, retirement benefits, lifetime earnings, pensions, asset building, housing, care for parents from the baby boomer generation, double care, loneliness and social isolation. The government's policies are too heavily skewed toward employment. Don't they need to be reconsidered?"

In response, Prime Minister Ishiba said: "We have placed emphasis on employment because we believe it occupies a central role, but regarding your point that policy is overly biased toward employment... I would like to hear what you believe should be improved and how, and I will respond as necessary while taking your comments into account." In other words, while acknowledging that the government had prioritized employment, he accepted the criticism and indicated a willingness to consider specific improvements.[^kokumin_kokkai]

### What the "Issue Map" Revealed About Policy Blind Spots

What this exchange demonstrates is precisely the function of the "issue map" explained in Chapter 3. However, simply creating an issue map does not automatically reveal policy problems. The key lies in the comparative work of setting the issue map against the government's existing policies.

**Visualizing the issues**: The challenges faced by the employment ice age generation were not limited to employment. There were many different issues, including retirement benefits, lifetime earnings, asset building, housing, parental caregiving, double care (raising children and caring for parents at the same time), loneliness, and social isolation. Broad listening made it possible to visualize this overall landscape of concerns in the form of a scatterplot.

**Finding "blind spots" by comparing with existing policy**: But simply looking at the issue map does not tell you what the problem is. What matters is comparing the issue map side by side with existing policy.

![Flowchart showing the process from collecting opinions to aggregation through broad listening and comparison with existing measures.](images/04_02_kokumin_broadlistening_hikaku_en.png)
*Figure: The process from collecting and aggregating opinions to creating an issue map and comparing it with the government's existing policies*

The government's support measures for the employment ice age generation were focused mainly on employment issues. By contrast, the issue map contained many clusters unrelated to employment, such as housing, caregiving, asset building, loneliness, and social isolation. Only by comparing the two does the problem become visible: the government's policies are overly concentrated on employment. The issue map shows the full picture of citizens' concerns, and the gap between that picture and existing policy brings the blind spots into view.

**Concrete policy proposals**: Based on the issues made visible in this way, Ito proposed specific policy measures. These included special hiring programs for employment ice age generation applicants in public-sector jobs such as teaching, making age-based wage increase rates at companies visible (through mandatory disclosure of human capital information), support for managing defined-contribution pensions, expanding catch-up payment systems for the National Pension and Employees' Pension Insurance, and redesigning guarantor systems for single people who are hospitalized.

### How Broad Listening Changed the Quality of Parliamentary Questions

Traditionally, questions in the Japanese Parliament were based on issues that lawmakers or political parties themselves considered important. But there was no guarantee that those matched the issues people were actually struggling with. Opinion polls can ask about preselected options, but if you do not know in the first place what should be asked, you cannot design appropriate survey items.

Broad listening solves this problem. By allowing citizens to speak freely and then framing questions around the issues that emerge from those voices, it becomes possible to clearly show the gap between "what people are actually struggling with" and "what the government is addressing."

Ito's questioning was not an attempt to measure public opinion in the sense of "what percentage of people think this." It was the presentation of issues: "there are people struggling from these perspectives." And it raised the question of whether those issues had been left out of government policy.

### What Was Happening Behind the Scenes of This Groundbreaking Parliamentary Question in 2024?

March 7, 2025, was the first time in Japan's Parliament that the results of broad listening were used as the basis for questioning the government. But this breakthrough did not appear out of nowhere. To understand it, we need to turn the clock back about a year.

On March 28, 2024, Takae Ito took the floor in the House of Councillors plenary session to explain her opposition to the budget bill. The moment she began recounting her own experience as a member of the employment ice age generation—"In 1997, when I was rejected by as many as 100 companies..."—laughter broke out in the chamber.[^kokumin_warau] The incident triggered a major reaction on social media, with many people from the same generation expressing anger. In response, Ito consulted party leader Yuichiro Tamaki (as he then was), and the party decided to take up policy for the employment ice age generation in earnest.

A project team chaired by Ito was launched, and in April 2024 the party conducted an online survey using Google Forms. In just eight days, it received responses from 933 people from the generation in question.[^kokumin_iceage] The results showed that 87.8% answered that they had either "not used" or "never heard of" the government's "Employment Ice Age Generation Support Program." The biggest challenges respondents reported were "insufficient preparation for old age," "financial hardship," and "having few people to rely on in an emergency." The survey also revealed that the disadvantages faced by this generation were not limited to the stereotypical image of "male, non-regular worker, socially withdrawn," but extended to women and full-time employees as well.[^kokumin_jiji] After repeated expert hearings and discussions among lawmakers, the party compiled a six-pillar policy proposal in June 2024 and submitted it to the Minister of Health, Labour and Welfare in September.[^kokumin_teigen]

In other words, even before adopting broad listening, the Democratic Party for the People had already been running a cycle of gathering opinions through surveys and hearings and turning them into policy.

### January 2025: The Introduction of Broad Listening

Broad listening using TTTC had already built up a track record through the July 2024 Tokyo governor election (see the previous section) and collaborative reporting with Nippon TV during the October 2024 House of Representatives election (see the next section). Against this backdrop, on January 16, 2025, Mr. Yasuno and his colleagues announced the "Digital Democracy 2030" project. The idea was ambitious: provide broad listening technologies such as TTTC and public-hearing AI as open source so that political parties and local governments could use them freely, and spread AI-driven political and administrative reform across Japan.

The Democratic Party for the People was the quickest to respond to this call. Just five days later, on January 21, 2025, Secretary-General Kazuya Shinba instructed House of Councillors member Takae Ito to join the initiative. Shinba praised the technology in glowing terms, calling it "exactly the right way to use AI. The fact that it can aggregate voices that are not right in front of us, hidden voices, and voiceless voices—this is a savior for democracy."[^kokumin1]

But implementation was not easy. One condition set by Yasuno was "to have a highly capable systems engineer available full-time."[^kokumin2] Ito visited her former IT company and secured an engineer who met the requirement. The presence of an engineer with advanced skills in GitHub, Python, TypeScript, and related tools proved to be the key to making broad listening work.

On January 24, 2025, the party's YouTube channel, "Go! Go! Kokumin Live," aired a special livestream featuring Yasuno.[^kokumin2] During the broadcast, Secretary-General Shinba spoke excitedly, saying things like, "Even someone like me who doesn't fully get it finds this exciting," and "They should make Mr. Yasuno the minister in charge of digital affairs." At one point he even declared, "Mr. Yasuno is a national treasure."

During that YouTube livestream, Ito candidly reported that, based on her actual experience using Talk to the City, there were five major barriers:

1. **The barrier of organizational decision-making** - In addition to explaining what broad listening is, there is the challenge of deciding who within the organization should use the visualized opinions and how
2. **The barrier of securing engineering talent** - It is necessary to secure full-time engineers with capabilities beyond those typically found among party staff or lawmakers
3. **The barrier of training the AI** - It requires patient tuning and cultivation, and prompt adjustment is difficult
4. **The barrier of money** - The protocol is open source, but collecting opinions from across Japan makes data collection and analysis costs enormous
5. **Addressing the digital divide** - In addition to X, YouTube, live chat, and Google Forms, efforts are needed to convert telephone audio into text

### February 2025: From a Pilot at the Party Convention to Questioning the Government in Parliament

To address these challenges, the Democratic Party for the People obtained the phone number "050-1722-5930 (Kokumin)" and adopted a system that converted callers' voices into text and fed them into the AI. It also sought to overcome the digital divide by using a variety of channels, including postal mail, email, and in-person interviews.

Then, on February 10, 2025, as part of its full-scale use of broad listening, the party launched a dedicated platform called the "Kokumin AI Project."[^kokumin7] The platform was designed so that only party members could submit opinions, allowing the party to gather members' views directly on the policy issues it should address.

For policy concerning the employment ice age generation in particular, a dedicated page was created to collect specific accounts of daily challenges and policy needs from party members.[^kokumin8] These opinions were then analyzed with TTTC together with the voices of ordinary citizens collected through the telephone hotline, Google Forms, social media hashtags, and other channels, and became the supporting material for the parliamentary questioning on March 7, 2025.

One distinctive feature of how the Kokumin AI Project operates is that it does not simply "ask once and stop." Instead, it repeats the cycle of collecting and analyzing opinions. At first, it gathers a broad range of views and uses TTTC to narrow down the hot topics. Ito described this as "increasing the granularity of the labels,"[^kokumin2] meaning that a broad theme such as "Shouldn't there be measures for the employment ice age generation?" gradually becomes focused into a more concrete policy discussion such as "Should a minimum guaranteed pension system be considered?"

Once the party presents its view on a narrowed-down issue in the form of a policy proposal, supporters then respond on the Kokumin AI Project platform by voting for or against it.[^kokumin7] By repeating this cycle, vague "problems people are facing" are brought onto the table of actual policymaking. In addition, because both the collected opinions and the party's views are made publicly viewable on the web, the transparency of the process is also ensured.

![The policymaking process of the Kokumin AI Project: a diagram showing the full cycle of collecting opinions from party members and social media, aggregating them through broad listening, comparing them with existing measures, voting for or against policy proposals, and publishing the party's views on the web.](images/04_02_kokumin_ai_process_en.png)
*Figure: The full process from collecting opinions to aggregation through broad listening, comparison with existing measures, refinement of policy, and publication*

At the party convention on February 11, 2025, a pilot demonstration was conducted using AI in real time.[^kokumin_taikai] From roughly 2,000 opinions collected in advance, three themes were selected—hay fever, mild poverty, and child-rearing—and the AI-generated aggregation and visualization results were presented at the venue. The Democratic Party for the People thus attempted something genuinely new: introducing some of the "opinions" and "ideas" gathered from voters at the party convention itself.

### From Parliamentary Questioning to a Comprehensive Policy Package

Then, about one month after the party convention, on March 7, 2025, Takae Ito entered the House of Councillors Budget Committee carrying an issue map generated by TTTC from the voices of the employment ice age generation collected through the Kokumin AI Project, the telephone hotline, social media, and other channels, and used it to question Prime Minister Ishiba. Only 45 days had passed since the party, inspired by the ideals of DD2030, decided to adopt broad listening. The speed with which the Democratic Party for the People independently built a broad listening system and put it to use in parliamentary questioning was extraordinary. This led directly to the scene described at the beginning of this section. The questioning also drew media attention, including a March 20, 2025 Tokai TV feature on "digital democracy."[^kokumin10]

The parliamentary questioning was not the goal but one stage in the policymaking cycle. On May 28, 2025, the Democratic Party for the People announced a comprehensive policy package for the employment ice age generation that also reflected the results of broad listening.[^kokumin_policy] Its contents directly addressed the "issues beyond employment" that had emerged in the parliamentary questioning. To address pension anxiety, the party proposed easing the requirements for catch-up payments into the National Pension and creating a minimum guaranteed pension system. To address caregiving issues, it proposed expanding support for carers. To address housing issues, it proposed abolishing age requirements for public housing. It also included measures that went beyond the conventional framework of "employment support," such as promoting hiring methods that do not begin with resumes and interviews, and introducing a jobseeker basic income.

In the 2024 survey, 87.8% had answered that they had "not used" or "never heard of" the government's support measures for the employment ice age generation. Broad listening went further, revealing the full landscape of issues by showing that people were struggling with matters beyond employment as well. First, the survey confirmed the existence of the problems. Then broad listening visualized the breadth of the issues and the bias in existing policy. Finally, those findings were translated into policy. The Democratic Party for the People's policy work on the employment ice age generation is therefore a case in which combining conventional opinion-gathering methods with broad listening increased the resolution of policymaking.

## The Democratic Party for the People Fully Deploys Broad Listening in the House of Councillors Election and Achieves a Historic Breakthrough

Following its success in parliamentary questioning, the Democratic Party for the People made full use of broad listening in drafting its electoral platform for the House of Councillors election as well. It used AI to analyze more than 60,000 opinions submitted to the Kokumin AI Project, compared them against policy proposals, and introduced mechanisms such as fact-checking, placing broad listening at the core of its election campaign. The details of that process will be discussed in Chapter 6 based on interviews with Ito.

In the House of Councillors election held on July 20, 2025, the Democratic Party for the People won 17 seats. This was a historic leap forward—more than quadrupling its four seats up for reelection. In the proportional representation vote, it won more than 7.62 million votes, the highest total among opposition parties.[^kokumin4]

At a press conference during the extraordinary session of the Japanese Parliament following the election, on August 1, 2025, Secretary-General Shinba announced: "The Democratic Party for the People had four seats up for reelection, and 17 candidates were elected. Together with those not up for reelection, that gives us 22 members, and including our parliamentary group, we now have a force of 25 in the House of Councillors." He emphasized that the party had grown to the point where it could independently submit budget-accompanied bills.[^kokumin5]

Following this result, on September 16, 2025, Takae Ito was appointed to the newly created post of "Head of Party Communications."[^kokumin6] This established a structure for overseeing the party's digital communication strategy as a whole, including broad listening. Broad listening was no longer an experimental initiative; it had become positioned as one of the party's core strategies.
