# Column: A Word Cloud Is Not Analysis

By: @tokoroten

English translation by Luke Closs

When local governments and companies report the results of open-ended survey responses, a "word cloud" often makes an appearance. A colorful graphic is included, and the report says, "We analyzed the voices of citizens." But can a word cloud really be called "analysis"?

![Example of a word cloud](../../column/02_political_wordcloud.png)

The figure above is a word cloud created from a fictional open-ended survey. Words such as "policy," "citizens," "necessary," "problem," and "countermeasures" appear in it. It looks polished, and it may somehow give the impression that "analysis" has been done.

But what can we actually learn from this word cloud? Do citizens support this policy or oppose it? What do they say the problem is? What kinds of measures are they asking for? None of that can be read from it.

What a word cloud does is extremely simple. It breaks text into words, counts how often each word appears, and displays more frequent words in larger font sizes. That is all. So while it can show "what topics are present," it cannot show "what opinions are being expressed."

![Comparison of a word cloud and a word frequency list: Ryunosuke Akutagawa's "Rashomon"](../../column/02_rashomon_wordcloud.png)

The figure above makes this even clearer. It shows a morphological analysis of Ryunosuke Akutagawa's "Rashomon," extracting nouns, verbs, and adjectives, and placing a word cloud (left) next to a word frequency list (right) ([Aozora Bunko](https://www.aozora.gr.jp/cards/000879/files/127_15260.html)).

Look at the list on the right. All the word cloud is doing is converting this word frequency list into font sizes. "Servant" appears 44 times, "old woman" 28 times, "corpse" 28 times, and so on.

So what does that tell us? Even if words like "servant," "old woman," "corpse," and "hair" appear together, the context is completely lost: that the old woman was pulling hair from corpses, that the servant saw this and ultimately stripped the old woman of her kimono and fled, and that the story explores the theme of human selfishness.

What makes this even more troublesome is that a word cloud can make people feel as though they understand something. It produces a colorful, visually appealing graphic, so it somehow feels like analysis has taken place. But in reality, all it provides is surface-level information about word frequency.

A word cloud is nothing more than a method for visualizing how often words appear; it cannot show the substance of the opinions themselves. In an era when it was not technically possible to analyze the meaning of natural language, it became widely used because it was one of the only practical ways to "automatically and easily visualize the voices of citizens." But with frequency analysis alone, it is extremely difficult to derive insights that can actually inform policy.

To put a word cloud in a report and say "we analyzed it" is to count word frequencies and then pretend that the opinions themselves have been understood. A word cloud is not "analysis" of open-ended responses; it is merely a "visualization of frequency." The broad listening discussed in this book aims to go beyond that—to understand and structure the meaning of people's opinions.
