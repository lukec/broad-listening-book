## What I Realized After Gathering 10,000 Voices

Shutaro Aoyama ([Plural Reality LLC](https://www.plural-reality.com/) founder / Columbia University, Computer Science & Political Science)


In July 2024, I was helping Takahiro Anno with his Tokyo governor election campaign. As part of the engineering team, I worked on developing “AI Anno,” creating an AI avatar that responded around the clock to voters’ questions via YouTube Live chat and phone calls.

![AI Anno’s results. On YouTube, it answered about 6,200 questions over 14 days, and by phone, more than 830 questions over 10 days.](../images/column_ai_anno_results.png)

We received about 8,100 questions, with a valid response rate of 77%. Through AI, we were able to absorb a volume of public voices that would have been physically impossible for a single candidate to hear directly. It felt meaningful.

After the governor election, Anno launched [Team Mirai (meaning “Team Future”)](https://team-mir.ai/) and decided to run in the 2025 House of Councillors election. In the [DD2030](https://github.com/digitaldemocracy2030) community, I worked on developing “Idobata,” a deliberation platform. The concept was simple: “Let’s build a system where all 100 million people in Japan can deliberate together.”

![Early concept diagram for the Idobata system. It uses a funnel structure to gather citizens’ voices from diverse channels such as X, YouTube, phone, and in-person interactions, with AI organizing the issues and connecting them to policy development on Discourse and GitHub.](../images/column_idobata_concept.png)

*Figure: An early concept diagram for the Idobata system. It uses a funnel structure to gather citizens’ voices from diverse channels such as X, YouTube, phone, and in-person interactions, with AI organizing the issues and connecting them to policy development on Discourse and GitHub.*

I was also involved in building the “interactive policy platform,” a system that allowed citizens to submit proposed changes to the electoral platform through chat. During the 17 days of the House of Councillors election campaign, 9,688 change proposals were submitted, and 346 were actually incorporated into the platform. These included adding subsidies for rubella antibody testing for the partners of people hoping to become pregnant, adding public funding for HPV vaccines for men, and updating measures to address the “age 18 wall” faced by children with disabilities. Citizens’ voices were literally rewriting the policy document. As a mechanism for “delivering voices,” it was clearly working.

### Beyond “Delivering Voices”

And yet one question kept nagging at me.

We gathered 9,688 proposals. We answered nearly 10,000 questions. But if you asked whether any of that had become material for the core political work of reconciling competing interests, the honest answer would be no.

We could create a map showing “these kinds of voices were out there.” But it was not material for deciding “so what should we do?” Most of what we collected remained at the level of fragmented expressions of position. People were offering opinions without fully digging into what they actually valued, what they could not compromise on, and what they were willing to trade off. So even when you gathered thousands of responses, each one still stayed in the realm of “I kind of feel this way.”

There is a huge gap between “delivering voices” and “connecting them to decision-making.” And simply saying, “Let’s scale deliberation up to 100 million people,” was not enough to close that gap.

### The Day Even Three People Couldn’t Move a Discussion Forward

What made me realize this was, unexpectedly, something inside our own three-person company.

It happened after I co-founded [Plural Reality](https://www.plural-reality.com/) with Shunsuke Takagi and Yui Nishimura. We disagreed over the UI for our product’s admin screen. One person argued from the standpoint of design philosophy, another prioritized the experience of first-time users, and I was focused on moving quickly with a prototype-first approach. All three positions were reasonable, yet the discussion went nowhere.

What I noticed, watching this unfold, was that none of us had actually been able to articulate what we ourselves wanted in the first place. We were all using the same word—“direction”—while talking about completely different layers of the problem. Even a comment like “there’s too much information” meant very different things to different people. For one person, it meant “none of this is unnecessary; it’s all needed.” For another, it meant “too much is being shown on the screen.” The source of the conflict was not disagreement in opinion, but a lack of self-understanding.

So we tried using Baisoku Kaigi, an AI-supported “fast-track meeting” tool we were building ourselves, on our own internal discussion. Each of us answered questions generated by AI.

![The Baisoku Kaigi response screen. Users answer prompts such as “When evaluating a UI, the highest priority should be ease of understanding for first-time users” on a five-point scale from strongly agree to strongly disagree.](../images/column_baisoku_input.png)

*Figure: The Baisoku Kaigi response screen. Users answer prompts such as “When evaluating a UI, the highest priority should be ease of understanding for first-time users” on a five-point scale from strongly agree to strongly disagree.*

Then something interesting happened. Once each of us had put our own thinking into words, the structure of the disagreement became visible.

![An “organizational analysis report on the UI discussion” generated by Baisoku Kaigi. It concludes that “the core of the conflict lies in the fact that, while everyone is aiming for the same goal of a ‘good UI,’ each person is working from a different map—that is, a different set of evaluation criteria.”](../images/column_baisoku_report.png)

All of us agreed that we should move in an agile way and that the first view should be simple. So although it looked like we were in conflict, the foundation was actually shared. On the other hand, we were cleanly split on whether to prioritize clarity or efficiency. The moment that structure became visible, the discussion started moving. We realized that if we could decide this one point, everything else could be built on top of agreement.

The problem in consensus-building was not scale. Even with just three people, we had not been able to articulate what we wanted.

### Self-Understanding as the Bottleneck

At Columbia University, I had been researching [how the quality of dialogue changes when AI is placed between people](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.18201). This was exactly the issue at the heart of that research.

The point is not for AI to speak in place of a person, but for a person to deepen their own thinking through dialogue with AI. A vague feeling like “I’m somehow concerned about the quality of school lunches” can be developed into something like: “Perhaps cost-cutting due to free lunch policies is affecting quality. If that is true, then we should improve quality even if it means partial user fees.” Only at that point does meaningful dialogue with others become possible.

In other words, at the very top of the consensus-building pipeline is self-understanding. Unless people understand their own position and can put it into words, everything downstream stalls: organizing issues, improving psychological safety, exploring third options—all of it. Conversely, if each person has at least some grasp of what they value, what they cannot compromise on, and what they are willing to trade off, then the downstream process tends to run relatively smoothly.

This is also closely connected to the themes being studied by the [Cybernetic Humanity team at Sony Computer Science Laboratories](https://cybernetichumanity.com/). When computers expand human action, how far can people still retain the sense that “this is my action”? It is certainly convenient when AI summarizes your views for you, but if that convenience comes at the cost of losing the feeling that those views are truly your own, then something essential has been lost. Supporting self-understanding while preserving a sense of agency: I believe that designing for both at once is the most important question in this field.

### From Scaling Up to Scaling Out

That is why our strategy changed.

“Scaling up” means involving large numbers of people at once: gathering the views of 100 million people, processing tens of thousands of proposals. That was the direction pursued by Idobata and the interactive policy platform.

“Scaling out” means enabling the same quality of dialogue to happen simultaneously in many different settings. Instead of relying on a single gifted facilitator, the goal is to create conditions where discussions can reach a certain standard anywhere, with AI support.

We got a real sense of this in Ota City’s Jibungotokaigi, a citizen meeting designed to make public issues feel personally relevant. Participants had explored their own positions through dialogue with AI before the meeting, so from the very start they were able to share common ground and reach a level of discussion deep enough to ask one another, “Is that a fact, or just an impression?” Twenty residents, all strangers to one another, were able to get that far in their very first session. Chapter 11, “Plural Reality,” introduces the Ota City initiative in more detail.

We founded [Plural Reality](https://www.plural-reality.com/) in order to keep testing this hypothesis in real-world settings. Building systems to “deliver voices” through DD2030 and Team Mirai became the foundation of the work I do now. But to connect those voices to decision-making, a process that supports each individual’s self-understanding turned out to be indispensable. I think this bottleneck became visible precisely because I stayed on the ground, directly involved in broad listening as a practitioner.

I have always started from the premise that conflict will never disappear. My thinking is close to the agonistic democracy of political philosopher Chantal Mouffe. What I am aiming for is not “arriving at the correct answer,” but lowering the cost of conflict. Let differences collide as they are, and draw better answers out of that process—without exhausting ourselves unnecessarily.

If I had to put it in one sentence, I want to create “a world where fighting can be just a little easier.” And right now, my working hypothesis is that the most effective lever for that is individual self-understanding.
