# Chapter 6: Using Broad Listening in National Elections

English translation by Luke Closs

## The Democratic Party for the People’s Elections and Broad Listening: An Interview with Takae Ito

In March 2026, I interviewed Takae Ito, a House of Councillors lawmaker, at the House of Councillors office building, along with Mr. Yamada of Santo General Planning, who was responsible for developing and operating the AI system. Based on that interview, this section describes how the Democratic Party for the People used broad listening in the July 2025 House of Councillors election and the February 2026 House of Representatives election.

The party’s broad listening initiative for the House of Councillors election proceeded in three stages: collecting opinions, analyzing them with AI, and reflecting the results in policy.

### Collecting Opinions: An Online-Offline “Melting Pot”

At first, the Democratic Party for the People considered purchasing all post data from X in order to capture even unspoken needs. However, as Ito explained, “When it came down to whether we could really pay 5 million yen a month, it was simply hard for a small party like ours to secure that kind of money,” so they had to abandon the idea.

Instead, they adopted a strategy of gathering data from every accessible channel—what they described as “consuming all the data within a three-meter radius.” Specifically, they collected voices from posts under the X hashtag `#国民民主党に伝えたい` (“What I Want to Tell the Democratic Party for the People”), YouTube comments, Google Form surveys, phone calls through a call center, comments received at street speeches, opinions shared in roundtable discussions, petitions, letters, and more—drawing from every possible source, online and offline alike. Looking back, Ito said, “Rather than casting a net over the open sea, it was more practical to ask: are there any opinions we’re overlooking on this particular issue?”

There had been an earlier incident that drove home the importance of this kind of “gap check.” Since 2019, Ito had worked on menstrual health policy and menopause-related policy, achieving some concrete results such as the establishment of a comprehensive women’s health center. But when those items were removed from the party platform on the grounds that they had already been achieved, the backlash was immediate: “Have you forgotten about us?” From the standpoint of policy management, it was natural to remove items once they had been accomplished, since policy achievements are finite. But for the people directly affected, it did not feel that way. This experience became the starting point for the idea of using AI to check for omissions in the party platform.

### AI Scoring: Matching 60,000 Opinions Against 300 Policies

For the July 2025 House of Councillors election, the party carried out a matching process between more than 60,000 collected opinions and its platform, bills, and related materials. The AI was fed more than 300 platform commitments since the party’s founding, submitted bills, and even every question asked by its lawmakers in the Japanese Parliament, then asked to score how well existing policy addressed each submitted opinion.

For example, an opinion such as “There needs to be discussion about the 1.03 million yen income threshold” would be matched with wage-increase-related policy at a score of 85 points. By contrast, a deeply personal statement such as “I want to die without causing trouble for anyone” would be judged as something barely captured by the party’s existing platform. With more than 300 policies and 60,000 opinions, the number of combinations ran into the hundreds of thousands or even millions. As Yamada put it, it was “an amount that would kill a human being” if done manually, and only became feasible because AI handled the processing.

When a single post contained multiple opinions, it was first broken down by topic, and each was then analyzed quantitatively as a separate opinion. The workflow was to generate a list of low match scores—that is, opinions not adequately covered by the existing platform—and present that list to Ito.

Simply bringing the AI’s analysis results into the party would not have worked on its own. Ito described herself as a “translator,” responsible for converting data into the language of politics in conversations with the chair of the Policy Research Council and the heads of various policy committees. “Translation is everything,” she said.

One of the policy issues brought into sharp relief by the scoring process was the “age 18 wall.” Ito explained it this way: “When children with disabilities turn 18, they lose their place to go.” After-school day services will care for them until around 6:30 p.m., but because those services are based on the Child Welfare Act, they can no longer be used after age 18. The next step, continuous employment support services (Type A and Type B), typically end around 3:30 p.m. This three-hour gap forces parents to quit their jobs or switch to shorter working hours. As Ito put it, “Parents raising children with disabilities know they themselves will die first. That’s why they work so hard, hoping to leave something behind for their child. And yet they end up having to quit.”

Ito herself is directly affected, saying, “I became a politician because my daughter had a disability.” But she also noted, “I had received letters about it, and people had come to petition me. But the cold reality is that not everything brought in through a petition becomes policy.” Through broad listening, however, the issue became visible as a “mass,” and, as Ito recalled, “we obtained quantitative data.” The “age 18 wall” became one of the party’s headline platform commitments and even led to the submission of a bill.

Other issues, such as “medical tourism countermeasures (proper administration of health insurance for foreign nationals)” and “advance care planning,” were also incorporated into policy through the same process.

The updated draft policy package, revised to reflect the AI scoring results, was then made public to party members and supporters through the Kokumin AI Project. They were asked to vote Yes or No on each item and provide free-text comments. A total of 882 people participated. More than 90 percent supported every item, but the free-text responses also included constructive feedback such as “this doesn’t go far enough.”

### Visualizing Campaign Issues During the Election Period

Broad listening during the election campaign captured how voter concerns shifted in real time. Over the course of the 17-day campaign, the main issues moved dramatically—from “rising rice prices” to “20,000-yen cash payments and tax cuts,” and finally to “the foreign national issue.” Opinions related to restrictions on foreign ownership of land could be seen gaining strength week by week in Talk to the City’s visualizations.

Ito said, “You could literally see the issues changing. The House of Councillors election was an extremely unusual election.” She added that in the subsequent House of Representatives election, almost no such issue shifts were observed. The result underscored the importance of using broad listening to keep tracking changes in campaign issues in an era when social media dynamics can shape election outcomes.

### The Challenge of Policy Fact-Checking

The opinion-policy matching system also led to another application. For example, when the false claim spread on social media that “the Democratic Party for the People has abandoned its policy of cutting the consumption tax,” the matching system could quickly locate platform commitments and Japanese Parliament questioning related to consumption tax cuts. Applying this mechanism, the party created a “party fact-check” system that could issue a rebuttal within two hours based on the previous five years of policy and Japanese Parliament questioning[^kokumin3]. It emerged from the recognition that “only we know what our correct position is” and that “by the time a Community Note appears, the election is already over.”

On the special website for the House of Councillors election, the party published Q&A-style responses to six disputed policy claims[^kokumin10]. At that stage, it did not directly quote posts by ordinary individuals, but there was still criticism of the very idea of a political party calling its own work “fact-checking,” and Ito recalled that “it caused quite a firestorm.” That experience led directly to “Kokumin Fact” in the House of Representatives election.

### A Party Culture That Accepts Market-In Thinking, and the Positive Cycle of Broad Listening

For broad listening to function within the party, there was a prerequisite more important than the technology itself. Ito explained it using the marketing contrast between “product-out” and “market-in.” A product-out party is one that formulates policy based on its ideals and ideology, then asks the public to support it. A market-in party, by contrast, listens to what the public wants and builds policy from there.

Ito said, “Traditional parties are product-out. But broad listening is a market-in approach, isn’t it?” She added that “there are remarkably few parties or politicians who can tolerate market-in thinking.”

As for why a market-in approach was possible in the Democratic Party for the People, Ito cited several factors: it is a party that does not “decide things based on ideology,” it has lawmakers from private-sector backgrounds so “methods that are standard in the private sector make sense to them,” and “party leader Tamaki himself is proactive in digital communication.”

In addition, it was crucial that at the start of the broad listening project, there was an initial agreement with party leadership that the AI’s analysis results would be reflected in policy all the way through to the end. “If the results come out and then, because they differ from someone’s personal views, they just get put in a drawer, that’s unacceptable. If that’s how it’s going to be, then I refuse to spend these resources, this money, or my own energy on it.” It was precisely because of that resolve that the effort did not end up as broad listening in name only.

### The February 2026 House of Representatives Election

The February 2026 House of Representatives election was a snap general election, and there was nothing like the preparation time available for the House of Councillors election. Both Ito and Yamada looked back candidly, saying, “We could hardly do anything,” and “We weren’t even able to collect new opinions.”

Within those time constraints, the main efforts were as follows.

The first was the policy fact-checking initiative “Kokumin Fact.” Despite the criticism during the House of Councillors election, the party decided, “We have to do this,” and tried again[^kokumin11]. This time, however, unlike the earlier Q&A format, the special site posted screenshots of ordinary users’ X posts along with their account names, which became a major point of controversy. Criticism poured in, calling it “public shaming of ordinary people” and “encouragement of online mobbing,” and there were reports that some of the individuals featured received aggressive direct messages from supporters[^kokumin12]. On February 5, three days before voting day, the issue became a major scandal covered widely by the media. Ito acknowledged “inappropriate operation,” deleted all the posts, and issued an apology[^kokumin13]. The episode once again highlighted the difficulty of political parties conducting fact-checking themselves.

The second was the use of broad listening for Kokumin Club, a platform for organizers. According to Ito, the Democratic Party for the People’s traditional information flow had been a B2B2C model: “party headquarters → prefectural federations (regional organizations) → local lawmakers → supporters.” As she explained, “Supporters connected to highly active lawmakers could get information right away, but offices and prefectural federations that were stretched thin created information gaps, and that became a source of frustration for people who wanted to help.” Kokumin Club was an attempt to bypass those middle layers and “develop a B2C or even C2C route,” using accumulated data to provide information directly to organizers and supporters.

At the same time, there were lessons learned. Ito reflected, “We put out dozens of policies, but ideally we should have released them in advance and had people score them. Because we didn’t have time, we asked for opinions on social media after the election had already started, and that triggered backlash—people asked what kind of party the Democratic Party for the People was even trying to be.” The inability to follow the process that had worked in the House of Councillors election—matching policy against public opinion in advance and checking for omissions—emerged as a key problem in the House of Representatives election.

Ito also pointed out that the electoral environment itself was unfavorable to broad listening. This House of Representatives election was even described as a “fandom election,” with fervent support for Prime Minister Takaichi, symbolized by “Sana-katsu,” taking center stage. “I myself understand the feeling of wanting Japan’s first female prime minister to do well, of not wanting to see her stumble,” Ito said. But in an election dominated by fandom, emotions shape voting behavior more than policy substance does. “How do you reach people who are struggling just to get through the present, who can’t talk about the future or policy, who aren’t interested in it, and don’t even have the room to think about it?” The election exposed a fundamental challenge: the broad listening approach itself—premised on matching policy with opinions—can be hard to make effective in a fandom-driven election.

### The Next Phase of Broad Listening

After two elections, broad listening was facing a new challenge. After more than a year of continuously collecting opinions through Talk to the City, the incoming voices had begun to converge, making it harder for genuinely new perspectives to emerge. Yamada frankly acknowledged that “the content being collected has started to become fixed.” Ito, too, asked, “What do you think the next use case is?”

Two possible directions emerged during the interview.

One is a Q&A system built on the accumulated data. The Democratic Party for the People has more than 300 policies, but even highly committed party members do not necessarily know all of them. As a result, exchanges like “Haven’t you done this?” “Actually, we have.” “Then isn’t this still insufficient?” happen frequently. Yamada said that by building the matching system used in the House of Councillors election to connect 60,000 opinions with policy, the party is beginning to create a situation where such questions can be answered immediately. In that sense, AI may eventually become a new channel connecting party members and supporters to policy.

In fact, there was already a precedent for this direction. In July 2024, an interactive AI called “AI Yuichiro,” trained on party leader Tamaki’s Japanese Parliament questioning and social media statements, was released and attracted more than 100,000 uses in just 10 days. However, because OpenAI’s terms of service prohibited use in political campaigns, it was forced into temporary suspension after only 24 days. As Yamada noted, “It’s great when the number of users grows, but when users increase, the costs visibly rise as well,” so cost remains an issue too.

The other direction is AI-driven deep interviews. Many of the voices gathered through broad listening are surface-level statements such as “I’m struggling with X” or “Foreign nationals make me angry,” and they do not reveal the underlying causes. If AI could continue the conversation by asking, “Why do you feel that way?”, it might be able to get at the root of problems that even the speaker had not fully recognized. During the interview, one proposal was to reconceive this as “online petitioning.” If AI could dig into the background of people who come forward with petitions, Ito responded positively, saying that “it would be easier to bring that kind of question to the executive meeting.”

Not just “collecting” opinions, but also “delivering” and “digging deeper” into the knowledge that has been collected. These ideas are still at the conceptual stage, but the accumulated practice of broad listening seems to be pushing the search for new uses.

### The Seamless Integration of Online and Offline Politics, and Broad Listening

Looking back on the two elections, Ito argued that one reason broad listening has become more important is the seamless integration of online and offline campaigning.

“In 2022, I was out on the street as a candidate myself, and I was doing online live streams every day. There was definitely a sense that online and offline were connected,” Ito recalled. But, she said, “It was the 2024 House of Representatives election when online and offline truly became completely seamless.”

As a result of that seamlessness, election outcomes are now shaped less by party organization and more by grassroots movements on social media. Ito gave concrete examples: “It started with Ishimaru, then the Democratic Party for the People, Sanseito, and now this Sana-katsu phenomenon.” She said that “the way policy issues change and the way votes move are like a completely different world before and after that shift.”

In such a world, parties cannot fight elections unless they themselves respond to the movements constantly emerging on social media. They need to understand in real time what is happening online and adjust their policy and messaging accordingly. In Ito’s view, that is why broad listening has become necessary. The two experiences—capturing real-time issue shifts in the House of Councillors election and confronting the wall of a fandom election in the House of Representatives race—brought both its potential and its limitations into sharp relief.

### Post-Interview Reflections

What struck me most strongly in this interview was how far the Democratic Party for the People’s broad listening had evolved beyond the use in Japanese Parliament questioning introduced in Chapter 4 of this book.

Traditionally, broad listening has been used to grasp mass trends by summarizing and clustering large volumes of opinions, helping campaigns decide what messages to emphasize. This trend-tracking type of broad listening was also used in the House of Councillors election discussed here, but it had evolved beyond taking a snapshot at a single point in time. Instead, it tracked the changing campaign issues in real time over the course of the election.

That said, trend analysis through clustering has a structural limitation. Majority views appear as large visible clusters, but minority voices often fail to form clusters at all and are buried in the summarization process.

What makes the Democratic Party for the People’s effort especially noteworthy is that it practiced a form of broad listening that surfaced buried minority voices through full-record matching. Rather than aggregating 60,000 opinions through clustering, it matched every single one individually against more than 300 policies. That meant that even if only one person had raised an issue, if it was not covered by existing policy, it would still emerge as a “low match score opinion.” Issues like the “age 18 wall,” which had been raised in petitions but had never been turned into policy, became visible for the first time through this full-record matching approach.

This went beyond simply identifying trends to win elections; it uncovered voices that existing policy had failed to capture. The fact that Ito herself is a parent of a child with a disability is probably not unrelated to this design philosophy. As a case showing that broad listening technology can be used not only to visualize the majority but also to visualize minorities, the Democratic Party for the People’s effort deserves special attention.
