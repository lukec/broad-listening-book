## 5.1 The Challenge of a City of 14 Million: The Shin Tokyo 2050 Project

On November 22, 2024, the Tokyo Metropolitan Government launched the [“Shin Tokyo 2050” Project, Created by Everyone](https://www.seisakukikaku.metro.tokyo.lg.jp/basic-plan/choki-plan/ikenbosyu). In this chapter, we refer to this initiative as Shin Tokyo 2050 (“New Tokyo 2050”). The initiative aimed to gather residents’ views broadly as part of formulating a new long-term strategy, the “Tokyo 2050 Strategy,” looking ahead to the 2050s. The strategy’s tagline was “Tokyo, Better Than Ever.” It was developed under a vision of realizing “Tokyo, the world’s greatest city,” where everyone can shine and each individual can feel happiness.

Traditionally, the standard way for government to hear from citizens has been the public comment process. But public comments have clear limits. Only a subset of highly engaged people tends to respond to formal calls for input, and the number of submissions often remains in the hundreds. In Tokyo, with a population of more than 14 million, it is unrealistic to claim that “public opinion” can be understood at that scale.

The Shin Tokyo 2050 Project took on this challenge using broad listening methods. During a roughly one-month submission period (November 22 to December 20), it collected 27,915 opinions—dozens or even hundreds of times more than a conventional public comment process.

Six channels were prepared for collecting opinions:

1. **Dedicated Google Form**: the most orthodox method
2. **X (formerly Twitter)**: posts using the hashtags “#シン東京2050” and “#百合子に言っといて”
3. **YouTube**: comments on Governor Koike’s press conference video
4. **Email**: the traditional method of sending opinions by email
5. **Postal mail**: to accommodate people less comfortable with digital tools
6. **Street interviews**: hearing directly from Tokyo residents in public spaces

What stands out most is the collection of opinions from social media. By analyzing hashtag posts on X and comments on YouTube, the project attempted to capture voices from people who would never voluntarily respond to a government call for comments.

Another notable point is that online recruitment brought in many younger voices that conventional public comment processes had failed to capture. The traditional pattern—government consultations being dominated by middle-aged and older respondents—shifted through the use of online channels.

At the same time, some voices cannot be reached through digital channels alone. Tokyo Metropolitan Government staff also visited Ueno Zoo on holidays and directly asked children for their opinions. Takahiro Anno, who provided technical support, commented that “one important thing is to diversify the data sources,” and a multi-angled approach combining online and offline methods was attempted[^ntv_bl].

[^ntv_bl]: NTV NEWS, “[Using AI to Visualize Public Opinion: Behind the Scenes of Tokyo’s Long-Term Strategy Development](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=b8-zEjbZZfk)” (2025)

On the technical side, analysis was led by [GovTech Tokyo](https://www.govtechtokyo.or.jp/), a general incorporated foundation established with full funding from the Tokyo Metropolitan Government. GovTech Tokyo is a specialized organization responsible for promoting digitalization across the metropolitan government and municipalities, with then–Deputy Governor Manabu Miyasaka serving as its chair. On the same day the Shin Tokyo 2050 Project began—November 22, 2024—Takahiro Anno was appointed as an advisor to GovTech Tokyo and supported the development of the broad listening system. Using the open-source opinion analysis tool [Talk to the City](https://github.com/AIObjectives/talk-to-the-city-reports), GovTech Tokyo built a system that clustered and visualized roughly 28,000 opinions with AI.

The collected opinions were [published as analysis results](https://broadlistening.seisakukikaku.metro.tokyo.lg.jp/20250131/index.html) on January 31, 2025, and were said to have been reflected in the “[Tokyo 2050 Strategy](https://www.seisakukikaku.metro.tokyo.lg.jp/basic-plan/2050-tokyo/)” formulated in March of the same year.

## 5.2 The Tokyo Metropolitan Government’s Official Account: From 20+1 Strategies to 28

According to the Tokyo Metropolitan Government’s official account, the results of broad listening took shape as a major reorganization of the policy framework. The [official website](https://www.seisakukikaku.metro.tokyo.lg.jp/basic-plan/2050-tokyo/) states:

> Through AI analysis, we identified the concerns and issues of Tokyo residents and revised the strategy’s policy framework.

In the earlier “[Future Tokyo](https://www.seisakukikaku.metro.tokyo.lg.jp/basic-plan/choki-plan/)” strategy (formulated in 2021), the framework consisted of “20+1” strategies. In the “Tokyo 2050 Strategy,” this was expanded to 28 strategies. These were organized under three pillars—“Diversity,” “Smart City,” and “Safe City”—plus “Other (cross-cutting fields).”

![Policy framework of the Tokyo 2050 Strategy](images/05_shintokyo_2050_result.png)

**Diversity**: Children, Child-Rearing, Education, Youth, Women’s Empowerment, Work Styles, Longevity, Community, Inclusive Society

**Smart City**: Startups, Digital, International Finance, Industry, Tourism, Culture & Entertainment, Sports, Urban Development & Housing, Infrastructure & Transportation, Greenery and Water

**Safe City**: Zero Emissions, Urban Resilience, Disaster Prevention, Urban Safety and Security, Healthcare

**Other (cross-cutting fields)**: Tama & Islands, Structural Reform, All Japan, Co-Creating the Future

The Tokyo Metropolitan Government explains that broad listening played an important role in this reorganization.

However, many outside commentators discussing this initiative focus on the fact that “tourism” became independent within the 28 strategies, and tend to frame it with a narrative like this: “A megacity like Tokyo discovered the theme of tourism. In other words, broad listening uncovered something that had been overlooked, and that changed policy.” Is that narrative true? The next section examines the question.


## 5.3 Verifying the Results: What Changed, and What Did Not

### The Problem of the Comparison Baseline

Let us pause here and examine the Tokyo Metropolitan Government’s claims. In its official account, it compares the “Future Tokyo” strategy formulated in 2021 with the 2025 “Tokyo 2050 Strategy.” On that comparison, the strategic framework certainly appears to have changed dramatically.

But to properly evaluate the effect of broad listening, we need to choose the right comparison baseline.

![Culture and entertainment city strategy in “Future Tokyo” as of 2021](images/05_tokyo_2040_2021.png)

*Figure: The “Future Tokyo” strategy formulated in 2021. Tourism was positioned as one policy measure within the “Culture and Entertainment City Strategy,” not as an independent strategic category. The context of the COVID-19 pandemic and the halt in inbound tourism is strongly reflected.*

The “Future Tokyo” strategy was formulated in March 2021. Recall what that moment was like. It was the height of the COVID-19 pandemic, and even the holding of the Tokyo Olympic and Paralympic Games was in doubt. Inbound tourism had effectively stopped, and even statistical surveys of foreign visitors to Japan had been suspended.

Given that context, it is only natural that “tourism” did not exist as an independent strategy in the 2021 version. While the tourism industry was suffering catastrophic damage during the pandemic, it would not have been realistic to make tourism promotion a central strategic pillar. That said, the 2021 version did not entirely lack tourism measures. It included tourism promotion in sparsely populated parts of Tokyo such as the Tama region and the islands. With inbound tourism unavailable, tourism policy was tied to regional revitalization within Tokyo itself.

In fact, the Tokyo Metropolitan Government updates the strategy every year as a “version up,” and in January 2024 it released “Future Tokyo Strategy version up 2024.” By that point, the emphasis of tourism policy had already shifted significantly toward urban tourism centered on central Tokyo, including nighttime tourism, anime tourism, and accessible tourism. This was a policy shift anticipating the recovery of inbound tourism.

![Culture and entertainment city strategy in the 2024 version up](images/05_tokyo_2040_2024.png)

*Figure: The version up released in January 2024. Policies anticipating the recovery of inbound tourism—such as nighttime tourism, anime tourism, and accessible tourism—were significantly expanded. This update came before broad listening was conducted (from November 2024 onward).*

*Source: [“Future Tokyo” Strategy](https://www.seisakukikaku.metro.tokyo.lg.jp/basic-plan/choki-plan/) (Tokyo Metropolitan Government Bureau of Policy Planning)*

In other words, the expansion of tourism policy was already underway before broad listening was conducted (November–December 2024).

| Comparison | Impression | Reality |
|------|------|------|
| 2021 version → Tokyo 2050 Strategy | Dramatic change | Includes four years of change: pandemic recovery, Olympics concluded, inbound tourism recovery |
| version up 2024 → Tokyo 2050 Strategy | Incremental update | The actual difference after broad listening |

Moreover, the real nature of the change in the strategic framework was not “new discovery” but “subdivision.” Tourism was split out from “Culture & Entertainment.” It is not accurate to say that “AI discovered a field the government had overlooked.” A more accurate description is that “fields previously grouped together were subdivided based on the distribution of opinions.”

### The Meaning of the Strategies That Disappeared

At the same time, some strategies from the 2021 version disappeared.

- **Strategy 0: Overcoming Infectious Disease** → removed as COVID subsided
- **Strategy 19: Olympic and Paralympic Legacy** → four years after the Games, integrated into areas such as sports

These changes are better understood as natural updates driven by changing circumstances than as the result of broad listening. As COVID recedes, the priority of infectious disease measures declines; once the Olympics are over, “legacy” is absorbed into other policy areas. Any local government would likely make similar updates.

Based on this examination, the Tokyo Metropolitan Government’s story that “broad listening changed policy” is, at least in part, overstated. But does that mean broad listening contributed nothing? No. The next section attempts a more accurate assessment—neither inflated nor dismissive.


## 5.4 What Broad Listening Actually Brought

### Capturing Voices That Had Not Been Heard

One value of broad listening is that it can capture voices conventional mechanisms had failed to hear. Which opinions make it into the policy process depends on how the collection mechanism is designed. Shin Tokyo 2050 can be understood as an attempt to consciously redesign that mechanism.

What deserves attention here is what is often called “silver democracy” in Japan: an age-skewed democracy in which older voters have disproportionate influence. The political influence of each generation in elections is determined roughly by “population by generation × voter turnout by generation.” In Japan, population aging means that older generations are already larger in number, and older people also vote at higher rates than younger people. This double effect creates a structure in which policy is easily pulled toward the preferences of older voters. In other words, it is a structure that tends to produce policies prioritizing elderly welfare.

For the vision of “a Tokyo where everyone can shine” to have real meaning, there must be a mechanism through which the voices of all those people can reach policy. But as long as that age skew remains, younger voices will struggle to be heard. Simply urging people to vote is not enough. More fundamentally, those under 18 do not even have the right to vote. The fact that children, who will live longest in the world of 2050, are institutionally excluded from decision-making about the shape of that society is a basic contradiction within democracy itself.

The Tokyo Metropolitan Government’s combination of collecting social media hashtags and interviewing children at Ueno Zoo can be interpreted as an attempt to address that age skew. Broad listening through social media and street interviews was, in this sense, an effort to bring into policy voices that the electoral system could not capture.

Put differently, by gathering opinions through channels with many younger users, the government can also be seen as trying to strengthen the legitimacy of future-oriented policy.

The published analysis results support this reading. In the education category, multiple clusters emerged around the future of children and young people, including “education reform,” “the future of education,” “strengthening multilingual education,” “delaying school start times,” and “measures against declining birthrates and support for child-rearing.” In the youth category as well, several clusters centered on “support for young people,” such as “measures against declining birthrates and youth support,” “supporting and empowering young people,” and “support for young people and coexistence with older generations.” In the final policy framework of the “Tokyo 2050 Strategy,” the Diversity pillar also positioned “Children,” “Child-Rearing,” “Education,” and “Youth” as independent strategies, reflecting this accumulation of opinions.

This was not accidental. In an age-skewed democracy, voices such as “make Tokyo easier for raising children,” “make education free,” and “make Tokyo a place where young people can dream” are hard to express with political force. Through social media and online channels, however, those voices arrived in large numbers. Parents actively raising children and younger generations, groups that had rarely responded voluntarily to government consultations, used X and the dedicated form to speak up. As a result, these voices became visible both in the number of submissions and in the number of clusters.

In other words, the issue areas of child-rearing, education, and youth support were not “new facts discovered by policymakers.” They were things that had long been understood, but whose scale had not reached government through the conventional public comment system. Tokyo’s broad listening can thus be seen as an attempt to correct that structural imbalance through the design of its channels.

### Serving as an Issue Map

Another value of broad listening is that it provides a structural basis for policy judgment. Its value does not lie in raw submission counts, but in clustering and structuring opinions. In other words, it lies in making the issue space visible. Intuition alone cannot explain to citizens why tourism should be positioned as an independent strategy. A numerical justification based on submission counts is also fragile, because it is directly shaped by bias in the collection channels. But the structure of opinions, including which subthemes exist and how they are arranged, can function as a basis for policy judgment.

This becomes concrete when we look at the [TTTC visualization results for the tourism category](https://broadlistening.seisakukikaku.metro.tokyo.lg.jp/20250131/tourism/index.html).

![TTTC visualization of the tourism category (Shin Tokyo 2050)](images/05_tttc_tourism.png)

The ten clusters include themes such as “an international tourism hub,” “a globally attractive city,” “digital tourism experiences,” “making the city more attractive as a tourism destination,” “integrating tourism and culture,” “entertainment in Odaiba,” and “developing water transport,” all of them related to promoting urban tourism. Particularly noteworthy is that “countermeasures against overtourism” appeared as an independent cluster. Expectations for tourism promotion and concerns about its negative effects coexist within the single theme of tourism. The coexistence of promotion and concern means that tourism is not a simple matter of support or opposition, but a theme containing multiple policy issues.

If these issue groups remain buried within the broader category of “Culture & Entertainment,” a policy intention such as overtourism countermeasures risks being obscured by other measures. To show how government intends to address tourism-specific issues, it becomes necessary to make tourism visible as an independent strategy. The answer to the question “Why was tourism positioned as an independent strategy?” then becomes not “because there were many submissions,” but “because tourism-specific issue groups had a structure that would become hard to see if absorbed into other fields.”

Connecting the issue space to the policy process was broad listening’s real contribution. In the Shin Tokyo 2050 Project, the [page for each strategy](https://www.seisakukikaku.metro.tokyo.lg.jp/basic-plan/2050-tokyo/shinchoku) in the “Tokyo 2050 Strategy” includes an “AI summary of Tokyo residents’ opinions,” making visible which public voices correspond to which strategy. This stands in sharp contrast to the conventional public comment process, which often ends with a formulaic line such as, “The comments received will be taken into consideration in future deliberations.”

### Inferring the Path to Independence

So why did the Tokyo Metropolitan Government come to position “tourism” as an independent strategy? Because it has not published the details of its workflow, what follows is necessarily speculative. The most plausible explanation is the relationship between the Tokyo Metropolitan Government’s organizational structure and its strategic framework.

A government’s strategic framework is closely tied to the jurisdictions of the departments responsible for it. The organization’s structure, meaning which bureau or division handles which field, is reflected in the strategy’s category system. In software engineering, this is Conway’s Law: organizations design systems that mirror their communication structures. The same dynamic operates in government strategic frameworks as well. The category system of the “Future Tokyo” strategy likely corresponded closely to the jurisdictions of Tokyo Metropolitan Government bureaus and divisions.

What broad listening did was cluster citizens’ voices and make the issue space visible independently of that organizational structure. When those clusters are compared against the existing strategic framework, some do not fit neatly.

Most clusters correspond to existing departments, reinforcing the impression that “this is all familiar.” But if a large number of tourism-related opinions are mixed into “Culture & Entertainment,” some clusters become difficult to map. “Countermeasures against overtourism,” for example, may have been one such cluster.

If that misalignment is left unresolved, it becomes difficult to claim that the strategy truly responds to the voices of Tokyo residents. So tourism is separated out. Creating an independent strategy also gives the organization grounds to build a corresponding structure on its side. When the strategy changes, room opens up for the organization to change as well.

AI did not propose, “Tourism should be made independent.” Rather, humans interpreted the friction between the AI’s classification results and the existing institutional structure, and posed the question: should this category be split out? The role of broad listening is not to “produce answers,” but to “provide material for asking questions.”

This question, moreover, was not a “dramatic discovery.” As discussed in Section 5.3, the expansion of tourism policy was already underway in the 2024 version up. But that is fine. Even a known issue, once structured as citizens’ voices, can provide grounds for revising the policy framework. For the decision to make tourism independent, broad listening adds the justification that “the structure of citizens’ voices points in that direction.” That view of the issue space functioned as a foundation of legitimacy in precisely that way.

That said, what it can show is only “the structure within the opinions submitted,” not “the distribution of opinion across society as a whole.” Structural grounds are more explanatory than intuition or raw counts, but when they are spoken of as if they prove the public will, the grounds become exaggerated.

## 5.5 What This Case Shows

As an early case of broad listening being introduced into Japanese public administration, the Shin Tokyo 2050 Project will become a reference point for future efforts. Its significance and its limits are both worth clarifying.

### The Risk of the Story That “Broad Listening Changed Policy”

There is also cause for concern. The Tokyo Metropolitan Government’s official account emphasizes that “AI analysis led to a revision of the strategy’s policy framework.” But as Section 5.3 showed, most of the change was driven by changing circumstances—the end of the pandemic, the recovery of inbound tourism—and by the accumulation of existing policy updates.

The story that exaggerates broad listening’s contribution is risky in two ways. First, it creates excessive expectations about technology. Second, it risks being used as an after-the-fact justification for policy shifts the government had already decided to pursue. If the explanation “we listened to citizens and changed course” is in fact no more than a post hoc endorsement of what was already going to happen, then transparency is not improved; it becomes less sincere.

If government is to use this technology in the future and claim a causal relationship—that “the results of broad listening changed policy”—then it will need to show that relationship in a verifiable way.

### As a Tool for Asking Questions

The role of broad listening is not “to produce answers,” but “to provide material for asking questions.” Shin Tokyo 2050 can be understood as an attempt to demonstrate that role in urban policy at the scale of 14 million people.

This too is speculative, but the scale itself likely made certain things visible. The misalignment between the issue space and the organizational structure likely revealed a problem: the issues citizens feel most strongly and the government’s vertical bureaucratic structure do not line up. The same may be said of this age skew in democracy. People likely sensed it already, but because it is difficult to discuss openly, the project can be read as an attempt to correct this structural problem through approaches such as social media and street interviews.

In both cases, this was not “the discovery of unknown facts,” but an approach to things people had already half understood. The story that “broad listening changed policy” is overstated, but the assessment that it “structured the questions and supported the legitimacy of change” does hold. The significance of posing those questions at the scale of 14 million people is something later cases will carry forward.
