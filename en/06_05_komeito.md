# Chapter 6: The Use of Broad Listening in National Elections

Author: tokoroten


## Komeito: Broad Listening Undertaken by a Governing Party

### Broad Listening by a Governing Party Facing Declining Youth Support

Komeito, the junior partner in Japan’s ruling coalition, faced a structural challenge: the aging of its support base. As the membership of Soka Gakkai, its main lay Buddhist support organization, grew older, the natural decline in elderly supporters continued, while support among younger generations failed to grow. In House of Representatives elections, its proportional representation vote had fallen from just under 9 million at its peak to below 6 million by 2024[^komei_vote]. Winning support from younger voters had become an urgent priority for expanding the party’s strength.

Against this backdrop, on March 17, 2025, Komeito launched the policy development survey “We Connect.” The project aimed to reflect the “unfiltered voices” of young people and working-age generations in the party’s priority policies for the House of Councillors election (voting and counting on July 20). The survey period lasted about three months, from March 17 to June 26. Hereafter, this section abbreviates it as “We Connect.”

At the time, Komeito was part of the ruling coalition with the Liberal Democratic Party. This distinguishes it from the other cases in this chapter—Team Mirai (meaning “Team Future”), Ishin, and the Democratic Party for the People—all of which were opposition parties. Komeito pursued broad listening from a position where it could actually implement policy.

This section is based not on direct interviews with Komeito, but on publicly available information such as the project website, the party’s official YouTube channel, and published materials.

### From Opinions to Policy: We Connect’s Double-Diamond Process

The policy development process for We Connect was explicitly published on the project website. Its distinguishing feature was that it was designed not as a one-time survey collection, but as a two-round cycle.

![Flow of policy development in We Connect](images/06_05_WeConnect_workflow.jpg)[^komei_flow]

First, the first survey was launched on March 17. The responses received from late April through the Golden Week holiday period were then aggregated and discussed within the party. After Golden Week in May, a second survey was conducted, and policy proposals were further debated based on the feedback gathered in the first round. The first survey remained open even after the second began, and individual investigations by lawmakers were also conducted in parallel with the surveys. Based on these layered discussions, the party’s Policy Research Council finalized policies in June, which were then announced as priority policies before the official start of the House of Councillors election campaign.

This process had a structure resembling the “double diamond” model known from design thinking. First, opinions were gathered broadly across 22 policy areas (divergence), then narrowed down to priority areas using AI (convergence), then gathered again (divergence), and finally turned into policy decisions (convergence). In other words, it involved two cycles of divergence and convergence. By using AI in the convergence phases, the system was designed to synthesize large volumes of opinions without relying solely on manual labor.

The use of AI to analyze the collected responses was clearly stated on the project website:

> “Using **AI and other digital technologies**, we will analyze the voices and requests submitted through the survey from multiple angles—for example, what kinds of people (by demographic attributes) are expressing what kinds of views and requests most often. By identifying the needs of young people and working-age generations, we will connect them to policy development.”

From the outset, the intention was not merely to collect opinions, but to use AI to understand who was saying what, by demographic group, and connect that to policy. Komeito’s declaration, as a governing party, that it would incorporate AI into the policy development process can be seen as one indicator that broad listening was entering the mainstream.

On the same day the We Connect survey launched, a short video explaining the project’s purpose was posted on Komeito’s official YouTube channel[^komei1].

> “When politicians discuss things only among themselves, the discussion tends to become biased toward immediate issues.”

> “We want people across the country—especially younger generations—to directly share with us voices such as ‘I want this to be achieved’ and ‘This is what I’m struggling with,’ and we will place those voices at the very center of Komeito’s policies and make sure they are realized.”

> “We would like to share the voices we receive from everyone [through the project website and social media]. Then, while receiving even more feedback such as ‘this is still lacking’ or ‘you should also do more like this,’ we are determined, as Komeito—a governing and responsible party—to ultimately bring it all together and make it happen.”

As this video shows, We Connect was designed around a model in which “the party gathers citizens’ voices, translates them into policy, and delivers results.” It was not a model of direct deliberation among citizens, but one in which the party stood at the center as intermediary, premised on its governing capacity to implement policy.

### The Collection Mechanism: Grassroots Dissemination Through Google Forms and QR Codes

The channel for collecting opinions was an input form set up on the dedicated website, “weconnect.jp.” It was implemented by embedding a Google Form, making it a simple setup that relied on existing tools rather than a sophisticated custom-built system. Anyone could respond, whether or not they were party members, and no email addresses were collected.

People were directed to this form through multiple routes: posts on Komeito’s official social media accounts (X, YouTube, Instagram, and others), QR codes printed in paper media such as the party newspaper and the *Komei Handbook 2025*, and grassroots outreach in which local lawmakers across the country directly introduced the QR code during street speeches and community activities. Social media’s role was limited to directing people to the form and providing interim updates; unlike some other parties, Komeito did not use methods such as scraping social media posts from X hashtags.

### What Did the 103,519 Responses to the First Survey Reveal?

The first survey, launched on March 17, 2025, had a three-part structure. First, it collected six demographic attributes: gender, age, area of residence, occupation, marital status, and whether the respondent had children. Next, respondents were asked to select up to three areas of interest from 22 policy fields, including “measures against rising prices,” “employment and labor issues,” “education and child-rearing support,” “housing,” “measures against loneliness and isolation,” and “coexistence with foreign residents.” Finally, respondents entered free-text answers to the prompt: “What issues are you currently concerned about, and what opinions, requests, or ideas do you have regarding politics and policy?” Because demographic attributes and areas of interest were collected together, cross-analysis such as “in their 30s, raising children, interested in employment” became possible.

By April 30, 91,216 responses had been collected, and by June 24 the total had reached 103,519. The collected responses were reportedly grouped and analyzed using AI. However, because the specific algorithms used were not disclosed, this book does not go into the technical details of the analysis and instead introduces the results only to the extent possible from public information.

March 2025, when We Connect began, was a time when Japan’s consumer price index (all items less fresh food) remained elevated at +3.2% year-on-year[^cpi], and rice prices had nearly doubled compared with the previous year[^rice]. In the midst of what was dubbed the “Reiwa rice crisis,” the government had conducted an unprecedented auction release of stockpiled rice just before We Connect began.

Reflecting this context, the word cloud of collected responses prominently featured terms such as “stockpiled rice,” “rising prices,” “taxes,” “tax cuts,” “policy,” “lawmakers,” “households,” “cash benefits,” and “support.”

![Word cloud of responses submitted to We Connect](images/06_05_WeConnect_wordcloud.png)[^komei3]

*Note: The Japanese character 米, meaning “rice,” appears prominently in this word cloud.*

In the results for selected policy issues (multiple answers allowed), “measures against rising prices and economic stimulus” was overwhelmingly the top choice at about 65,000 responses, followed by “social security such as pensions, healthcare, and nursing care” (about 50,000) and “education, child-rearing support, and measures against the declining birthrate” (about 25,000).

What is notable, however, is that “political reform, including the issue of money in politics,” ranked relatively low. Since its founding, Komeito has promoted “clean politics” as a core part of its identity, and in the 2024 revision of the Political Funds Control Act it led efforts to strengthen so-called joint liability provisions and lower the disclosure threshold for party ticket purchasers[^komei_reform].

Even so, the voices of more than 100,000 voters concentrated on issues directly tied to daily life, such as rising prices and social security, while interest in the banner Komeito had carried for 60 years was relatively limited. Broad listening thus also served to make visible the gap between the party’s preferred message and voters’ actual needs.

![Number of selections by policy issue](images/06_05_WeConnect_policy_issues.png)[^komei3]

Cross-tabulation by age group showed that “measures against rising prices and economic stimulus” was the top concern across every generation, from teenagers (47.5%) to those aged 70 and over (71.3%). By contrast, differences emerged in “pensions, healthcare, and nursing care”: interest was higher among older groups—people in their 50s (53.0%), 60s (69.2%), and 70 and over (71.0%)—but relatively low among teenagers (23.0%). “Education and child-rearing support” peaked among people in their 30s (48.5%) and 40s (43.5%), matching the realities of the child-rearing generation.

![Cross-tabulation of age group × policy issue](images/06_05_WeConnect_age_policy.png)[^komei3]

By crossing generation with areas of interest, the survey design appears to have aimed to visualize “what policies younger people truly want” and thereby gain clues for winning their support.

### The Second Survey: Moving into the Convergence Phase

The second survey differed significantly from the first in its question structure. Demographic information was reduced to just two items—gender and age—while area of residence, occupation, marital status, and whether the respondent had children, all collected in the first survey, were omitted. Since the demographic analysis had already been completed in the first round, the second survey appears to have prioritized lowering the barrier to response.

The core of the survey also changed. Whereas the first survey was a divergent question asking respondents to choose “areas of interest” from 22 policy fields, the second was a convergent question in which respondents were shown six concrete policy proposals, developed by the party based on the first-round responses, in a visual card format and asked to “select the policy proposals you would like to see promoted.” The six policy proposals were as follows:

- **[Support for rising prices]** Measures to reduce the burden of rice and food costs, cash benefits, lower gasoline prices, subsidies for electricity and gas bills, etc.
- **[Social security]** Income compensation during caregiving leave, subsidies for nursing care facility fees, housing support, etc.
- **[Education]** Expanded loan-forgiveness programs including for graduates, expansion of grant-type scholarships, etc.
- **[Child-rearing]** Free medical care for children, improving the quality of public education, improving the environment surrounding children, etc.
- **[Employment and labor]** Expanding work-style options suited to different life stages, a four-day workweek, etc.
- **[Employment and labor]** Major wage increases for childcare workers and care workers, improvements in workplace conditions, etc.

![Policy selection screen for the second We Connect survey](images/06_05_WeConnect_survey2.png)[^komei3]

Each policy proposal was presented visually as a card with a catchphrase, in sharp contrast to the plain checkbox list of 22 fields used in the first survey. The second survey received 23,147 responses, and among them, “Support household finances—act now” won support from about 80% of respondents.

### Reflection in Policy

After the survey period ended, Komeito published on the We Connect website an explanation of how the collected voices were reflected in its priority policies for the House of Councillors election[^komei_manifesto], under the heading “Young people’s voices became our priority policies for the House of Councillors election”[^komei3]. The main reflected policies were listed as follows:

| Theme | Main reflected policies |
|---|---|
| Household support | Income tax cuts (raising the basic deduction), cost-of-living support payments (e.g., ¥40,000 for households with children under 18 and low-income households), summer reductions in electricity and gas charges, tax cuts for car users and abolition of the provisional gasoline tax rate, increased rice production and price stabilization |
| Nursing care | Higher incomes for essential workers (appropriate reflection in official reimbursement rates), support for working caregivers (stronger consultation systems and promotion of caregiving leave), medical and nursing care DX (ICT adoption and online consultations) |
| Education | Scholarship tax relief (income deductions for repayment amounts, relaxed income limits for reduced repayment programs, expanded proxy repayment systems) |
| Child-rearing | Free school lunches, free high school tuition, support for university tuition, free pregnancy and childbirth care, universal childcare access, support for school refusal, promotion of 35-student classes, creation of a housing allowance, promotion of parental leave uptake, expansion of after-school childcare measures |
| Work styles | Raising the minimum wage to a national average of ¥1,500 in the 2020s, equal pay for equal work (abolishing the “regular/non-regular” distinction), reskilling support, review of the ¥1.06 million and ¥1.3 million income thresholds, employment support for older adults |
| Childcare workers and care workers | Raising pay to the average level across all industries (¥386,000 per month) |

AI was also used to communicate these priority policies. An overview of the policies was created and published using the AI presentation tool “Gamma”[^komei_gamma]. Policy proposals do not speak for themselves once drafted; they must be communicated through a variety of information channels. For that reason, it is important to convert policy proposals into forms suited to each channel. Gamma appears to have been useful in turning policy proposals into clear visual materials.

![Overview page of Komeito’s “2025 Priority Policies” created with Gamma](images/06_05_WeConnect_gamma.png)[^komei_gamma]

In addition, the digital edition of the *Komei Shimbun* included an AI policy search feature titled “Find the Komeito platform for you with AI!”[^komei_search]. Users could enter questions in free text, and the AI would identify and explain the pledge among the 60 priority policies that was closest to their interests. Internally, this likely used vector search, a method based on semantic similarity between texts. For individual policies, there was also a button labeled “Ask AI why this policy is necessary,” allowing users to hear the policy background from the AI.

One of We Connect’s major distinguishing features was that it incorporated AI at every stage of the process: collecting opinions, analyzing them, developing policy, and communicating it to voters.

### Combining Party Organization and Broad Listening

Compared with the other cases in this chapter, Komeito’s We Connect had a somewhat different character in several respects.

First, scale. Compared with Team Mirai (8,559 responses) and the Democratic Party for the People (60,000), the combined total of about 127,000 responses across the first and second surveys was the largest in this chapter. However, this scale likely owed much to Komeito’s nationwide network of local lawmakers distributing QR codes through grassroots efforts. Its collection structure differed from that of Ishin, which gathered opinions from an unspecified mass of social media users (300,000 responses collected).

Second, collection method. Ishin used social media scraping, Team Mirai used pull requests via AI interviews, and the Democratic Party for the People used multiple online and offline channels. Komeito, by contrast, chose the simple tool of Google Forms. Its distinctive feature was not technical complexity, but the way it combined existing organizational strength with existing tools to achieve large-scale opinion collection.

Komeito has long presented “the ability to listen to small voices” as one of its strengths. We Connect can be seen as an attempt to update that traditional stance into a contemporary form using digital tools.

In the end, however, in the July House of Councillors election Komeito won only 8 seats out of the 14 it was defending, and its proportional representation vote fell to 5.21 million (down 970,000 from the previous election). The party’s post-election review document described the situation as “a crisis threatening the party’s survival” and cited as a cause of defeat its “failure to expand support among working-age voters in their 40s and 50s and younger voters in their teens through 30s”[^komei_soukatsu]. Despite collecting about 127,000 voices through We Connect, the party did not succeed in winning greater support from younger generations.

The causes of electoral defeat cannot be explained by broad listening methods alone, but We Connect appears to have had structural limitations. The method of distributing QR codes through the party organization’s grassroots network became the driving force behind collecting more than 120,000 responses, but it may also have limited the reachable audience to the party’s existing support base. It remains unclear how many voices were included from the “young people with no prior connection to Komeito” whom the party most needed to win over. This case suggests that the success or failure of broad listening depends not only on the sophistication of the tools, but also on the structure of who can actually be reached.

At the same time, the review document explicitly states that future party reforms will include the introduction of “AI agents capable of real-time information sharing” and consideration of “strengthening programs to improve the quality of speeches and social media messaging”[^komei_soukatsu]. The fact that the party announced a shift of personnel and budget toward digital initiatives and openly acknowledged the need to strengthen its social media outreach lies on the same trajectory as We Connect’s attempt to build contact with younger people. In addition, the scholarship tax relief policy that emerged from We Connect was adopted as one of the party’s priority policies for the House of Councillors election. While We Connect did not translate into success in electoral results, it did leave behind two outcomes: a direction toward embedding AI in the policy development process, and the achievement of reflecting policies born from voters’ voices in the party platform.
