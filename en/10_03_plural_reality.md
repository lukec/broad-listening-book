## Introduction

This article introduces Plural Reality, an initiative that emerged through activities in Japan’s digital democracy and Plurality communities. Through its philosophy, solutions, and track record of support, we explain how broad listening technology is transforming the practice of consensus-building.


## Introducing Plural Reality

[Plural Reality LLC](https://www.plural-reality.com/) (Representative: Shunsuke Takagi; Founder: Shutaro Aoyama) is a consulting and software development company established to bring knowledge about broad listening, from Japan and abroad, into society.


Founder Shutaro Aoyama has worked on putting digital democracy into practice through activities such as “[Digital Democracy 2030](../11_00_DD2030による広聴AIの開発活動)” (DD2030) and “[Team Mirai (meaning “Team Future”)](../06_01_チームみらい)” (see Chapter 6). Online dialogue tools he developed in that process, including “Idobata,” drew attention as attempts to listen deeply to many people’s voices at the same time. Representative Shunsuke Takagi has also participated in digital democracy communities in Japan and abroad as a visiting researcher in the Digital Democracy Division of the SmartNews Media Research Institute and as the founder of Plurality Tokyo.

Through these activities, we came to feel keenly that while academic and community discussions were active, there was still a major gap between those discussions and making them usable in real-world settings. Communities such as “Digital Democracy 2030,” which also serves as the foundation for this book, play a major role. At the same time, there also needs to be an organization that can turn ideas into concrete services and help them take root in practice. Plural Reality was founded to combine the ideals of digital democracy with insights gained from the practice of consensus-building, and to make them truly usable in society.


We believe that staying close to clients’ real-world needs and responding carefully, one step at a time, including the painstaking work, is exactly what is needed to make digital democracy take root in Japan.

### Seeing Consensus-Building as Humanity’s Enduring Problem

Plural Reality believes that humanity’s enduring problem is consensus-building. Humanity began by beating one another with wooden clubs, and may yet bring about its own end by using nuclear weapons over territorial disputes. All of these are problems caused by failures of consensus-building.

No matter how far technology advances, the human challenge of “aligning perceptions (consensus-building)” and the “decision-making” that depends on it will remain. Plural Reality believes that by rebuilding current institutions on the basis of LLMs (large language models), AI technology capable of processing and generating human language, it is possible to expand the range of outcomes in consensus-building and decision-making so that all stakeholders can benefit more.

## Solutions We Provide

Through consulting and software, Plural Reality supports efforts to rethink how consensus-building is conducted in a wide range of settings, from resident assemblies run by local governments to decision-making within companies.


Plural Reality believes that consensus-building has a common underlying structure.

1. Clarify the interests of all participants
2. Place those interests together on a single table as an interest map and extract the key issues
3. Present several options (while also sketching the likely future under each option)
4. Communicate with one another and bring about compromise and behavioral change
5. Make a decision

To support this process, a neutral mechanism that does not favor any particular position is essential. Ideally, within a transparent and simple framework that everyone can accept, differing opinions can be turned from sources of conflict into drivers of progress.

The software provided by Plural Reality uses AI to make it possible to understand each person’s views more broadly and more deeply. By combining these tools with consulting, the company provides end-to-end support for consensus-building processes, from design through implementation.

### Baisoku Kaigi: Redesigning Surveys and Interviews with AI

![The Baisoku Kaigi landing page. Alongside the tagline “Accelerate issue extraction,” it shows a smartphone response screen. The page explains: “What we accelerate is not meetings, but the process of reaching consensus. Before the meeting, we visualize every member’s level of support and concern so discussion can begin with the issues already clarified. This is an AI tool that supports advance preparation and alignment of understanding.”](images/10_03_baisoku_lp.png)

Traditional surveys and one-on-one interviews have long sat at opposite ends of a tradeoff. Surveys are static, asking the same questions of many people. One-on-one interviews can dynamically adapt questions to each individual and their background, but they take time. Decision-makers have always been forced to choose between these two extremes.

![The “dilemma of aggregating opinions” faced by government. A diagram showing the tradeoff between surveys (quantity) and in-person workshops/public hearings (quality). The conventional limitation was that if you prioritized “quantity,” “quality” declined, and if you prioritized “quality,” you could not achieve “quantity.”](images/10_03_tradeoff_dilemma.png)

[Baisoku Kaigi](https://baisoku-kaigi.plural-reality.com/gov) (“accelerated meeting”), an AI-supported “fast-track meeting” tool provided by Plural Reality, overcomes this tradeoff by redesigning surveys, interviews, and alignment of understanding around AI.

Baisoku Kaigi was designed to enable augmented deliberation, based on founder Aoyama’s experience developing online dialogue tools such as “Idobata.” Augmented deliberation is an effort to expand human deliberative processes with the help of AI so that more people can participate in deeper dialogue.

![Plural Reality’s approach, titled “Our Approach.” The diagram shows the overall picture of AI conducting “deep-dive interviews” with thousands of people simultaneously. It illustrates a flow from many person icons through an AI agent to generated outputs, and explains three steps: “AI dialogue generation: AI generates follow-up questions in real time based on participants’ responses, enabling individually optimized dialogue. It also detects emotions from the core and filler of responses”; “Automatic structuring: Natural language is automatically structured into ‘issues,’ ‘support/opposition,’ and ‘conditions,’ and patterns of conflict are detected. The range of possible agreement is visualized”; and “Integrated outputs: Achieves interview-level quality at survey-level speed. Automatically generates structured reports directly connected to policy recommendations.”](images/10_03_baisoku_approach.png)

Baisoku Kaigi is an AI survey, AI interviewing, and AI facilitation support tool that provides the following functions:

- **Align participants’ understanding**: Visualize differences in background knowledge among participants and establish a common starting point for discussion
- **Capture subtle nuances behind responses**: Increase the resolution of complex situations such as “support in principle, oppose in practice”
- **Support articulation**: Help people clarify ways of thinking they had not yet been able to put into words through dialogue with AI

It is used as follows:

1. Set “what you want to decide”
2. Enter background information on the issue
3. AI generates questions, and each participant answers them
4. Based on response patterns, AI changes the questions to probe each participant’s views more deeply
5. Each respondent’s understanding is displayed in a report (including points of agreement, points of difference, and points no one understands)

![The Baisoku Kaigi session creation screen. The administrator sets “what we want to decide” as the session title and enters background information. A preview on the right shows the format of the questions generated by AI. By setting background information in advance to align participants’ prior knowledge, the system enables AI to generate context-sensitive questions dynamically.](images/10_03_baisoku_setup.png)

![The Baisoku Kaigi administrator dashboard. The response progress of 37 participants in the session is displayed in real time. The overall completion rate is 89.5%, and each participant’s response status can be checked individually with a progress bar. A QR code is shown on the right, allowing participants to join the session simply by scanning it with their smartphones. Administrators can watch this screen and adjust the flow of the meeting according to participants’ response progress.](images/10_03_baisoku_dashboard.png)

Decision-makers can make judgments based on this report, and if there are gaps in understanding, participants can also discuss them with one another. In other words, Baisoku Kaigi is a platform that realizes broad listening itself.

![Visualization of response opinions by Baisoku Kaigi. Based on participants’ responses, the system automatically extracts and displays the “Top 3 by agreement,” “Top 3 by conflict,” and “Top 3 by ‘don’t know / not confident.’” Highly agreed-upon views can be shared as premises for discussion, highly conflictual views can be treated as issues requiring focused discussion, and views with many “don’t know” responses can be used to identify areas where more information is needed. Each view is linked to the Yes/No/Don’t know voting ratio and free-text comments.](images/10_03_baisoku_consensus_output.png)

## Case Studies

### Collaboration with Koso Nippon: Improving Randomly Selected Citizen Assemblies

One of Plural Reality’s partner organizations is Koso Nippon, an independent policy think tank.[^kosonippon] Its randomly selected resident assemblies[^minipublics] are conducted under the name Jibungotokaigi, a format designed to make public issues feel personally relevant. These assemblies have been held for more than 30 years, in 156 municipalities nationwide and more than 355 times in total, with participation exceeding 12,000 residents.


Plural Reality does not contract directly with municipalities; instead, tools such as Baisoku Kaigi are used in municipal settings through Koso Nippon. By providing technical support for the resident assemblies organized by Koso Nippon, Plural Reality also contributes to improving Koso Nippon’s own operational efficiency.

As mentioned in Chapter 2, there had been a tradeoff between the cost of organizing randomly selected assemblies and the amount of information they could gather. Plural Reality succeeded in overcoming this tradeoff by supporting the use of digital democracy technology in in-person meetings.

The introduction of Baisoku Kaigi to the “Jibungotokaigi” in Ota City, Gunma Prefecture, is discussed in detail in Chapter 8.

#### Yosano Town, Kyoto Prefecture: Supporting a Resident Assembly for Comprehensive Plan Formulation

In Yosano Town, Kyoto Prefecture, the “Yosano Future Conference” was held during fiscal year 2025. This conference was a forum for residents to help create the framework of the Third Yosano Town Comprehensive Plan, which will follow the Second Yosano Town Comprehensive Plan ending in fiscal year 2026. As population decline and aging continue, the goal was not for the government alone to envision the town’s future, but to share accurate information with residents and, through dialogue, imagine what the town should look like 10 years from now.

Invitations were sent to 5,000 randomly selected residents aged 16 and older living in the town, and participants were chosen from among those who applied. The conference was held over four sessions (September to December 2025), with discussions divided into three themes: “creating jobs,” “building everyday life,” and “developing people.” Koso Nippon supported the overall facilitation and operations, while Plural Reality provided AI-based technical support.

![The venue for Yosano Town’s “Yosano Future Conference” (Jibungotokaigi). On a large screen on stage, an opinion cluster map generated by a public listening AI is projected. Color-coded dots show the distribution of opinions, with topic labels such as “The challenge of youth outmigration and sustainable economic development in the region,” “Promoting regional revitalization and youth participation,” and “Declining willingness to participate due to unclear communication and lack of information.” A facilitator is explaining the results while operating a PC. A banner reads, “Your voice will shape Yosano’s future: Yosano Future Conference.”](images/10_03_yosano_meeting.jpeg)

A distinctive feature of the Yosano Town case was the major role Baisoku Kaigi played in the process of compiling the outcomes of discussion into a proposal for the local government. Using meeting transcripts and participants’ opinion submission sheets from each session as inputs, AI generated a draft proposal. This made it possible to organize residents’ statements into structured proposals under the headings “what residents can do,” “what the community can do,” and “what the government can do,” and to compile them in a form that could be used as the framework for the Third Comprehensive Plan.

In addition, opinions gathered during the meetings were analyzed and visualized by the public listening AI. In the second “creating jobs” group session held on October 13, 2025, 415 opinions were extracted from 384 comments and structured into 7 major topics and 49 subtopics. Issues grounded in local realities emerged, including “Promoting employment and resident participation through regional revitalization and diverse approaches” (97 items) and “Constraints on employment opportunities and working conditions due to regional characteristics and industrial structure” (82 items).

They are classified into seven topics, including “Promoting employment and resident participation through regional revitalization and diverse approaches” (97 items), “Constraints on employment opportunities and working conditions due to regional characteristics and industrial structure” (82 items), “Promoting regional revitalization through use of local resources and improved information sharing” (74 items), and “Exploring sustainable responses to changes in regional industry and social structure” (65 items). This initiative was carried out within the “Jibungotokaigi” framework organized by Koso Nippon and was designed to gather residents’ views on regional revitalization.

On an online platform called the “Higashiosaka Jibungotokaigi AI Discussion Space,” people could submit views on the theme “building a city where children and young people can realize their dreams,” including citizens who had not been able to participate in the meetings.

### Use in the Core Loop Project at DD2030

Plural Reality’s Baisoku Kaigi has been used not only in resident assemblies run by local governments, but also in projects within [Digital Democracy 2030](../11_00_DD2030による広聴AIの開発活動) (DD2030). In DD2030’s “Core Loop” project, Baisoku Kaigi was introduced into a policy discussion setting.

In the Core Loop project, discussion centered on a “push-based social security system,” specifically whether the government should proactively notify people in need of support by using data it already holds. Participants accessed Baisoku Kaigi from their smartphones and expressed their level of agreement on a five-point scale with statements such as: “I support the government using data it holds on income and family composition, without obtaining the individual’s separate consent, to provide support information through notifications (push-based guidance).”

![The Baisoku Kaigi response screen in the DD2030 Core Loop project. On the theme of a “push-based social security system,” participants respond to opinions on a five-point scale: “Strongly agree,” “Agree,” “Don’t know / not confident,” “Disagree,” and “Strongly disagree.” At the bottom of the screen, additional opinions generated by AI based on other participants’ response patterns are displayed, and participants answer them one after another.](images/10_03_dd2030_baisoku_ui.png)

Once responses are completed, each participant receives a “My Report (a self-understanding report for deliberation).” This report positions the participant’s response tendencies in comparison with those of other participants and visualizes on which issues they belong to the majority and on which they belong to the minority. Before entering discussion, participants can objectively grasp their own position, helping them articulate why they think as they do.

![The “My Report (a self-understanding report for deliberation)” generated by Baisoku Kaigi for each participant. An individual’s response tendencies are analyzed in comparison with those of other participants, and points of agreement, conflict, and uncertainty are organized. By objectively understanding their own position before discussion, participants can engage in more constructive dialogue.](images/10_03_dd2030_jibun_report.png)

The response results were analyzed in real time, making visible the issues on which agreement had formed among participants, the issues on which conflict had emerged, and the unknown areas where no one felt confident.

![Visualization of response opinions in the DD2030 Core Loop project. The display shows the Top 3 by agreement (for example, 100% support for “I support the government accumulating and using historical data on what support a person has received in the past in order to optimize future support”), the Top 3 by conflict, and the Top 3 by “don’t know / not confident,” making the priority of issues for discussion clear.](images/10_03_dd2030_consensus_analysis.png)

Participants shared comments such as the following:

> I was truly impressed by the “Baisoku Kaigi” system used in the Digital Democracy 2030 project. I really felt that the speed of deepening discussion became “double speed.” A simple AI meeting record only organizes what was said after the fact, but this works in real time, so AI can be used to organize and deepen discussion itself. It’s not just a replacement for someone taking minutes; it can even replace facilitation.

As this comment suggests, the value of Baisoku Kaigi lies not merely in automating meeting records, but in structuring and deepening discussion in real time. Whereas AI minute-taking tools remain at the level of “after-the-fact organization,” Baisoku Kaigi raises the quality of discussion itself by visualizing the issues during the discussion and creating an environment in which participants can always grasp what is being discussed at that moment.

The 75 collected opinions and 1,593 votes were structured and analyzed into 6 major topics and 21 subtopics using “Sensemaker” (see Chapter 10), an open-source opinion analysis tool developed by Google Jigsaw.


![The overview screen of the Sensemaker report for the DD2030 Core Loop. ### Use in Companies

Plural Reality’s work is not limited to resident assemblies run by local governments. “Idobata,” developed by founder Aoyama, is also being applied to consensus-building within companies. At Cybozu, for example, Idobata was introduced in August 2025 in a workshop on the theme of “promoting AI use within the company,” involving about 50 participants, and in just five minutes all opinions were structured into six key issues (see Chapter 9 for details).

## Conclusion

Plural Reality is working to implement knowledge about broad listening from Japan and abroad and to solve problems in every kind of consensus-building setting, from resident assemblies run by local governments to decision-making within companies.

Through AI support centered on Baisoku Kaigi and Idobata, it has succeeded in overcoming the challenge of asymmetry in participants’ background knowledge and in building a foundation for discussion. This has made it possible to overcome the conventional tradeoff between preserving the representativeness that comes from random selection and ensuring depth of discussion.

Going forward, Plural Reality will continue expanding the possibilities of new LLM-based approaches to what it sees as humanity’s enduring problem: consensus-building.
