## Turning Connective Action into Power

By @tokoroten


In this chapter, we have examined a range of broad listening tools and practices, including Polis and Taiwan’s vTaiwan. What common problem are they all trying to solve? As a framework for thinking about that question, the two movement types proposed by political scientists W. Lance Bennett and Alexandra Segerberg in their 2012 paper “The Logic of Connective Action” are highly useful.[^1]

Because *collective* and *connective* sound similar, they are often confused. In this book, we will render *Collective Action* as “organizational action” and *Connective Action* as “networked action.” Note that what this section borrows from Bennett and Segerberg is only the conceptual distinction between these two movement types. The later discussion of bargaining power, institutional linkage, and the four-quadrant model is the author’s own analysis.

Polis developer Colin Megill created Polis after witnessing the Arab Spring and Occupy Wall Street. Vast numbers of people gathered through social media and had a major social impact. And yet why did that not lead to institutional change? This question lies at the foundation of the design philosophy not only of Polis, but also of the practices discussed in this chapter, such as vTaiwan. Social media pile-ons have the same structure. Even when many people express anger and raise awareness of a problem, that rarely leads to a concrete solution. The concept of Connective Action clarifies this gap and helps explain why broad listening is needed as a mechanism for linking fluid public opinion to institutions.

### Two Types of Movements

**Collective Action** is a movement led by formal organizations such as labor unions and political parties. People first belong to an organization, and the organization invests money and personnel to build a shared collective identity.

Take a labor union strike, for example. If an individual goes on strike alone, they lose income during that period. In most cases, they cannot bear the economic pain and are forced to return to work without having their demands met. A labor union, however, can support striking members through a strike fund built from union dues. This allows members to continue striking without bearing the full economic burden. And because a labor union has representatives, it can both “start” and “end” a strike. It is precisely this capacity for control that gives the organization bargaining power with management. A deal becomes possible: “If our demands for better treatment are met, we will end the strike.”

As an extreme example, one could also point to the IRA (Irish Republican Army). The IRA was an armed organization that sought the unification of Northern Ireland (part of the United Kingdom) with the Republic of Ireland. In Northern Ireland, conflict between Protestant unionists (the majority), who wanted to remain part of the UK, and Catholic nationalists (the minority), who sought unification with Ireland, escalated from the late 1960s onward. This produced a de facto civil war known as “the Troubles,” which lasted for about 30 years. More than 3,500 people, including civilians, were killed in a three-sided cycle of violence involving IRA terrorism, retaliation by Protestant paramilitary groups, and repression by the British military.

What matters here is that this is not some distant historical episode. The conflict did not truly come to an end until the 1998 Belfast Agreement, and the IRA did not formally declare an end to its armed campaign until 2005. In the United Kingdom, a highly developed Western European country, armed conflict continued until just 20 years ago. What makes the IRA interesting as an example of Collective Action is that, as an organization, it was able to declare a ceasefire and agree to disarm. Even violent activity, if organized, can be “brought to an end.” That is why it becomes a source of bargaining power. This is the defining feature of Collective Action.

**Connective Action**, by contrast, is a very different type of movement. People do not belong to an organization; instead, they decide individually to participate based on what they see and hear on social media. Its defining feature is self-organization through loose networks temporarily connected by shared sympathy.

The Arab Spring of the 2010s is a classic example. The protest movements that spread in rapid succession from Tunisia to Egypt, Libya, and Syria expanded almost instantly through social media, and in Egypt and Tunisia they succeeded in toppling governments. But while they succeeded in launching the movements, they struggled to build channels for negotiation, decision-making, and institutionalization, and they failed to achieve stable transitions. The reasons were complex and varied by country, including political structures, the role of the military, and sectarian conflict, but the absence of a representative negotiating counterpart is widely cited as one contributing factor. In Egypt, the military staged a coup; in Libya and Syria, the situation descended into civil war. Movements that are weak in designing for closure and institutionalization can end in many ways: fragmentation, exhaustion, radicalization, and more.

The 2011 Occupy Wall Street movement in New York followed a similar pattern. Thousands of people protesting inequality occupied Zuccotti Park and spread the message “We are the 99%” around the world. But the movement had no leaders and no channel through which to negotiate with government or financial institutions. In the end, it was forcibly cleared by police, and its direct policy impact was limited.

Because loosely connected networks that arise spontaneously on social media have no organizer, they have no ability to control the movement. People who gather in anger may be able to force politicians or the media to recognize a problem, but that does not mean they can solve it. A lack of control means a lack of bargaining power. In other words, they cannot make the deal, “If our demands are met, we will end the movement.”

### The Structural Problems of Connective Action

Connective Action through social media has three fundamental dysfunctions.

First, the **absence of leadership** makes negotiation impossible. If there is no leader, there is no one who can negotiate on behalf of the movement. Even if the other side accepts the demands, there is no guarantee the movement will end. Under those conditions, the other side has no incentive to come to the table.

Second, such movements are **easy to hijack**. An unorganized movement is an ideal “vehicle” for forces that are already organized. There are two patterns of hijacking.

One is hijacking by activists or influencers who make a living from social issues. If their influence or revenue base depends on the continued existence of the issue, then the structure may incentivize the perpetuation of anger rather than the resolution of the problem.

The other is hijacking by existing political organizations. When people angered by a social issue begin demonstrating, it is common for left-wing groups focused on causes such as “anti-war” or “anti-nuclear,” or right-wing groups advocating things like “anti-immigrant exclusion,” to join in and redirect the movement. Some such organizations even adopt a strategy of “gaining legitimacy by being repressed by the government.” For them, it is more convenient for the movement to be suppressed than for the issue to be resolved.

Third, **social media platforms are incentivized toward “outrage,” not “resolution.”** Social media algorithms are designed to maximize engagement—likes, reposts, comments. Posts that inflame anger spread more easily, while calm posts offering solutions are buried. For the platform, it is more convenient for outrage to continue than for a problem to be solved. And so, whenever we look at our phone screens, we are continually recommended the latest outrage.

As a result, Connective Action falls into a double dead end. As long as it remains online, institutions can ignore it as “a large number of voices with no negotiating counterpart.” If anger escalates into demonstrations or direct action, it then provides a pretext for exclusion by being labeled a “mob,” while the substance of the issue is shelved. Whichever way it turns, the movement exhausts itself without reaching institutional change. That is the structural problem of Connective Action.

### Mapping Movements in Four Quadrants

The diagram below organizes the relationship between Collective Action and Connective Action. The vertical axis runs from “negotiable” (high institutional linkage) to “confrontational” (low institutional linkage), while the horizontal axis runs from Collective Action to Connective Action.

![Diagram of the relationship between Collective Action and Connective Action](images/11_05_collective_connective_action_en.png)

- **Upper left** (Collective Action + negotiable): This is where political parties, labor unions, and industry associations are located. They pursue their goals through formal political procedures such as parliamentary activity, collective bargaining, and petitions.

In Japan, shunto, Japan’s annual spring wage negotiations, is a classic example. RENGO (the Japanese Trade Union Confederation) sets target wage increases, affiliated unions negotiate with management, and if no agreement is reached, they go on strike. The power to “start” and “end” that strike is precisely the source of bargaining power. At first glance, a workers’ strike may look confrontational, but it is an exercise of the “right of collective action” guaranteed by Article 28 of the Constitution of Japan, and is therefore positioned within the institutional order. Because it is a legally recognized right, management has no choice but to come to the table.

Industry associations operate through the same structure. The Japan Medical Association sends members to the Central Social Insurance Medical Council, an advisory body of the Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare, and directly influences the setting of reimbursement rates for medical services, which are revised every two years. Member physicians are also local voters, and their organized voting power is a source of influence over politicians. Agricultural cooperatives function similarly. During negotiations over the TPP (Trans-Pacific Partnership), they strongly opposed the elimination or reduction of agricultural tariffs and, through coordination with politicians tied to agricultural interests, had major influence over the course of negotiations.

- **Lower left** (Collective Action + confrontational): This quadrant contains organized Collective Action that uses confrontational means, including armed struggle. The IRA and al-Qaeda are examples.

In Japan, the Japanese Communist Party during the era of its 1951 platform fits here. It adopted a line of armed struggle, organized rural guerrilla units, and engaged in direct action such as the Bloody May Day Incident of 1952. It later repudiated that line at the Sixth National Conference in 1955 and shifted to parliamentarianism, moving from the lower left to the upper left.

Aum Shinrikyo is an example of the exact opposite trajectory. After fielding candidates including Chizuo Matsumoto (Shoko Asahara) in the 1990 House of Representatives election and seeing them all lose, it abandoned the parliamentary route and accelerated its militarization. It manufactured sarin and explosives, leading to the Matsumoto sarin attack in 1994 and the Tokyo subway sarin attack in 1995. Whereas the Communist Party moved from the lower left to the upper left, Aum Shinrikyo attempted the upper left (parliamentary participation), failed, and fell into the lower left (armed struggle).

- **Lower right** (Connective Action + confrontational): This is where leaderless, spontaneous movements are located. They may begin with social media outrage or hashtag campaigns and develop into demonstrations or riots, as in the Arab Spring or Occupy Wall Street.

As noted above, movements in this quadrant have the power to raise issues socially, but they do not have bargaining power. Because they have no leaders, they cannot make the deal, “If our demands are met, we will end the movement,” which makes it difficult to connect them to institutional change. #MeToo and #BlackLivesMatter significantly shifted public opinion, but their connection to institutional reform varied greatly depending on the country and context. In Japan, the large-scale social media reaction to the former Unification Church issue in 2022 also required the intervention of existing party organizations—actors on the “left side” of the chart—before it could lead to concrete legislation.

Taiwan’s Sunflower Student Movement (see Section 10.1) is an exceptional case that escaped this quadrant. Students who had gathered spontaneously through social media occupied the Legislative Yuan, and figures such as Lin Fei-fan emerged as leaders, establishing channels for negotiation with the government. Occupying the legislature has the same effect as a strike: it halts national politics. A negotiation became possible—“If our demands are accepted, we will end the occupation”—and the movement moved from the lower right to the upper right. Another key to its success was the 22-day livestream by g0v, which ensured transparency and prevented the government from using violent removal.

- **Upper right** (Connective Action + negotiable): This is the domain where unorganized, fluid public opinion is connected to institutions. In the diagram, it is labeled a “mediation mechanism.”

What belongs here is not a tool, but **process and institutional design**. Polis, TTTC, and AI-powered public listening are tools for collecting and visualizing opinions, but they do not in themselves guarantee the upper right quadrant. vTaiwan belongs here because beyond the opinions gathered through Polis, it has designed channels for negotiation with government and linkage to legislation. In the Uber regulation case discussed in Section 10.1, more than 4,000 opinions were structured through Polis, points of agreement supported by both the taxi industry and Uber users were identified, and regulatory policy was then decided through face-to-face discussion among stakeholders. Taiwan JOIN’s rule that “5,000 supporters obligates a government response” is another example in which the connection from opinion gathering to government response is built into the institution itself. Likewise, the anti-fraud advertising law that followed a mini-public was not a permanent institutional mechanism, but it still belongs in the upper right as a case where citizen deliberation was connected to legislation.

In other words, tools perform the function of “gathering voices,” while institutional design provides the channel for “receiving voices.” Only when both are present can voices from the lower right reach the upper right. Even if AI-powered public listening and broad listening initiatives by local governments and political parties aim for this quadrant, if the institutional side is not designed to receive them, the visualized opinions will remain suspended in midair.

The constitutional guarantee of freedom of association, incidentally, is the right that makes possible a move from the lower right to the upper left—that is, allowing people with shared concerns to form organizations and gain bargaining power. But organization takes time and money. Making it possible to connect fluid public opinion to formal channels without going through organization—in other words, enabling a move from the lower right to the upper right—is, in the author’s view, one of the roles digital democracy is expected to play.

### The Structural Limits of Petition Tools: The Case of Change.org

As a digital tool aiming for the “upper right,” Change.org attracted attention early on. That is because it uses the formal route of petitions to connect fluid public opinion to politics. According to its official website, it has more than 570 million users and is widely recognized as “the tool people want to use when something goes wrong.”

But because of its design characteristics, Change.org in practice remains a confrontational method of “overwhelming by force of numbers” (the lower right). More than that, its promotion features make it possible to “buy” exposure for petitions, meaning that the more money one spends, the easier it becomes to gather signatures. Under this structure, the number of signatures does not necessarily reflect the strength of public opinion. In addition, technical safeguards for one-person-one-signature identity verification are insufficient, and the system allows signatures from multiple accounts.

Moreover, the figure “X hundred thousand people signed” is inherently newsworthy, and media coverage brings in still more signatures. Those news articles are then shared on social media as “evidence,” further accelerating the outrage. The visibility of signature counts easily creates a positive feedback loop of outrage, and as a result, the platform is often used as a tool of cancel culture.

In 2023, for example, a signature campaign called for the cancellation of the Japanese release of the film *Barbie*. It began after the official U.S. account reacted positively to fan art themed around the atomic bombings, but the petition campaign escalated beyond demanding an apology and moved toward demanding that the film itself not be released.

In 2024, another petition campaign sought to halt the release of the game *Assassin’s Creed Shadows*, gathering more than 100,000 signatures. It began with dissatisfaction over the game’s historical accuracy, but the campaign developed not toward solving the problem (correcting the historical depiction), but toward excluding the product itself.

What these cases have in common is that the petition campaigns were aimed not at “solving the problem,” but at “excluding the target.” A tool that was supposed to help solve problems instead became a tool for deepening division. That is the challenge with Change.org.

That said, this problem is not unique to Change.org. It is common to petition tools in general that operate through the structure of “gathering numbers to apply pressure.” The interaction between visible signature counts and media coverage, the paid promotion structure, and the lack of one-person-one-vote guarantees—these design characteristics remain essentially the same even if the platform changes. The very mechanism of digital petitioning contains a dynamic that pulls it not toward the upper right, but toward the lower right.

### Advances in AI Turn Free-Text Responses into Policy-Relevant Insight

The problem with Change.org is that it can present only the “power of numbers.” Broad listening, by contrast, presents not “numbers” but “structure.”

Traditionally, the data considered persuasive in policymaking has mainly been yes/no voting data. Quantitative data such as “60% in favor, 40% opposed” is easy to use as a basis for policy decisions, while free-text responses have often been dismissed as something that “cannot be quantified.”

What TTTC and AI-powered public listening do is transform such free-text responses into “policy-relevant information.” Imagine, for example, that 100 people in a workshop each express their views in their own words. Taken as-is, those are merely 100 separate individual opinions. But with broad listening tools, it becomes possible to visualize the structure: what themes those opinions fall into, how many opinions belong to each cluster, and which comments best represent each cluster. This is the process of turning scattered opinions on social media into a “proposal document.” “Noise” becomes “signal.”

Audrey Tang has said of TTTC: “Back in 2014, even with the power of g0v, it was impossible to interview participants in mini-publics and synthesize their views while preserving nuance. But now, with the help of Talk to the City, that cost has fallen to nearly zero.”[^2] With the arrival of LLMs, large-scale structuring of free-text responses—once impossible—has become feasible.

### Augmented Deliberation as a Mechanism of Connection

Where Change.org seeks to overwhelm the other side with the “power of numbers,” what vTaiwan and JOIN offer is structured information showing “here is where agreement exists,” along with an institutional channel for receiving it. For politicians, the former is a loyalty test of “are you with us or against us,” while the latter can be treated as “a solvable problem.”

The structural problem organized in this chapter’s four-quadrant model is also the problem that the enormous energy generated by Connective Action has no exit. If it stays online, it is ignored; if it takes to the streets, it is excluded. What breaks through this dead end is a design for connection from the lower right to the upper right.

That connection has three levels of depth.

1. **Broad listening as a tool** (Polis, TTTC, AI-powered public listening). These tools structure voices and generate information showing that “points of agreement exist,” but whether they are connected to institutions depends on how they are used.
2. **Broad listening led by politicians or government agencies.** This moves closer to the upper right through the intention that “those who listen will respond,” but because it is not institutionalized, continuity is not guaranteed.
3. **Support for augmented deliberation** (processes such as vTaiwan’s sequence of online opinion gathering → offline stakeholder coordination → policy decision, or JOIN’s obligation to respond). Because response is built into the institution itself, this stably realizes the upper right.

Broad listening dramatically lowers the cost of responding to citizens’ voices. Until now, politicians who could truly “hear” public voices were limited to those with strong local machines and large support organizations, or exceptional figures with a charismatic ability to read public sentiment. Broad listening tools make this responsive capacity available to anyone.

In this way, the three forms of broad listening can each be understood as attempts, in different ways, to carry voices from the lower right to the upper right.

Whenever an online pile-on erupts, I hope tools like Polis, TTTC, and AI-powered public listening will be used. That is the author’s wish. The goal is to direct the energy of anger not toward amplifying conflict, but toward discovering points of agreement—and then connecting those points of agreement to the policymaking process.

Connecting fluid public opinion to formal policymaking processes without allowing Connective Action to be hijacked by particular organizations: this is one reason why broad listening in the form of augmented deliberation became necessary. Extracting points of agreement from thousands of free-text responses once required hundreds of hours of human labor. In the 2020s, LLMs dramatically reduced that cost, making broad listening a practical way to link fluid public opinion to the policy process. As a receptacle for the enormous energy generated by Connective Action in the social media age, broad listening emerged almost inevitably at the moment when technological maturity and social demand converged.
